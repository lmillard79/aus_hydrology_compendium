## Detailed Analysis

**The integration of environmental objectives into Water Resources Management has shifted from simply allocating minimum passing flows to treating the environment as a legitimate, active water user** [1, 2]. With the acquisition of large volumes of water entitlements by government agencies (such as the Commonwealth Environmental Water Holder), environmental managers are now tasked with actively managing a complex "water portfolio" [1, 3]. This requires new paradigms in hydrologic modelling to ensure water is delivered to multiple environmental assets to maximize ecological outcomes, while also balancing the needs of consumptive users [2, 4].

**Defining Environmental Water Requirements (EWRs) and Ecological Responses**
To effectively manage environmental flows, planners use Environmental Water Requirements (EWRs) as a crucial link between River Systems Models and Ecological Response Models (ERMs) [4]. Rather than simply dictating a minimum flow volume, **modern EWRs utilize complex flow metrics—such as magnitude, frequency, timing, duration, and the sequencing of floods and droughts—to simulate natural conditions and maintain healthy aquatic ecosystems** [5, 6]. 

By defining specific EWRs, managers can categorize the state of an ecological asset's hydrological regime as optimal, sub-optimal, or detrimental [7]. For example:
*   An *optimal* regime fully supports the life cycle of the asset.
*   A *sub-optimal* regime allows individuals to persist but may halt reproduction.
*   A *detrimental* regime risks the death of the asset [8]. 

To monitor these ecological responses in real-time, bioindicators are frequently used. In the MacKenzie River, for example, algae and biofilm communities are monitored because they respond rapidly to flow disturbances and changes in water quality, providing an early warning system for managers making real-time flow release decisions [9, 10].

**Advanced Modelling and Optimisation Tools**
The complexities of Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM) have necessitated the development of advanced tools, such as the eWater Source modelling platform, which integrates environmental demands, water quality, and ecosystem health into a single suite [11, 12]. 

Because environmental demands often exceed available water, **optimisation models have been developed to serve as transparent, systematic tools that assist portfolio managers in making complex trade-off decisions** [1]. These models help managers decide whether to release environmental water, carry it over to the next season, or trade it, based on antecedent ecological conditions, climate, and system constraints [13, 14]. Multi-objective optimisation algorithms (such as the NSGA-II) are also applied to discover globally optimal operating rules that improve the reliability of environmental flows without compromising the supply to consumptive users [15, 16]. 

A unique challenge in modelling these systems is "environmental accounting." Unlike irrigation water that is extracted and metered at a farm gate, environmental water is delivered into a river reach and continues to flow downstream [17, 18]. Models must therefore untangle how much of the gauged river flow should be debited as environmental usage amidst natural inflows, losses, and existing minimum flow rules [17, 19]. 

**Insights from Specific Environmental Studies**
The sources highlight several specific studies where targeted modelling has been used to assess and protect ecosystems:
*   **Murray-Darling Basin Wetlands:** The MDB contains numerous sensitive, RAMSAR-listed wetlands that rely on specific inundation frequencies. Because passive satellite remote sensing struggles to map flood extents under dense vegetation canopies, hydrodynamic models combined with high-resolution LiDAR elevation data are used to rapidly map flood inundation depths and extents, allowing river managers to optimize the delivery of environmental flows to riparian ecosystems [20, 21].
*   **Flinders and Gilbert Catchments:** In assessing the sustainability of proposed agricultural irrigation, specific environmental studies evaluate the impacts of creating new reservoirs. These studies examine risks such as cyanobacterial blooms, greenhouse gas emissions, dissolved oxygen dynamics, and the impact of reduced streamflow on critical dry-season refugial pools for aquatic fauna [22, 23]. 
*   **MacKenzie River:** This study investigates configuring consumptive water transfers to achieve ecological benefits. By timing reservoir releases for consumptive use as "pulses" or "freshes," managers aim to mimic natural flow regimes, scour biofilms beneficially, and top up critical refuge pools, creating a cooperative approach with multiple socioeconomic and environmental benefits [5, 24, 25].
*   **Hawkesbury-Nepean River:** Facing urban growth pressures, linked catchment, hydrodynamic, and water quality models (like TUFLOW/FABM) are used to simulate biogeochemical processes and assess the water quality impacts of differing environmental flow regimes and wastewater servicing options [26, 27].
