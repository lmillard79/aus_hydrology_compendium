## Detailed Analysis

The research presented at HWRS 2024 highlights a significant paradigm shift in how extreme flood events are understood, estimated, and managed. Driven by recent catastrophic floods and the undeniable impacts of climate change, the symposium emphasizes the urgent need to move away from traditional, stationary modelling approaches toward dynamic, probabilistic, and multi-disciplinary frameworks. 

**The Record-Breaking February 2022 Floods**
The February 2022 floods in North-East NSW and South-East Queensland serve as a critical focal point in recent flood research, recognized as one of the most extreme events in Australian history regarding specific peak flow [1, 2]. **This event was characterized by unprecedented rainfall totals (nearly 800mm in 24 hours), highly saturated antecedent soil conditions, exceptionally fast flood propagation times, and multi-peak streamflow responses** [3-5]. The extremity of this event has prompted calls to revise Intensity-Frequency-Duration (IFD) curves and incorporate multi-peak design hydrographs into standard flood management practices [5, 6]. Additionally, the unprecedented water levels (such as the 14.4 m peak in Lismore) have spurred structural vulnerability assessments to better understand how buildings withstand extreme hydrostatic and hydrodynamic loads during rare floods [4, 7, 8].

**Climate Change and Hydrological Non-Stationarity**
A dominant theme at HWRS 2024 is that **historical assumptions of hydrological stationarity are no longer valid for design flood estimation** [9, 10]. Research indicates a dichotomous trend in flood peaks due to the competing forces of climate change: **while frequent floods (e.g., 1 in 10 AEP) are showing a decreasing trend due to drier antecedent catchment conditions and higher rainfall losses, rare extreme floods (e.g., 1 in 100 AEP) are increasing significantly (up to 26%) because the intensification of extreme rainfall outweighs these losses** [11-15]. 

To address this "deep uncertainty," researchers are advocating for "bottom-up" (or scenario-neutral) event-based modelling frameworks to test catchment sensitivity to changing rainfall extremes and losses [16-18]. Furthermore, new Australian Rainfall and Runoff (ARR) Climate Change guidelines recommend specific temperature scaling for different storm durations and introduce processes for adjusting losses and temporal patterns to account for these future climate scenarios [19, 20].

**Extending the Historical Record with Paleo and Indigenous Data**
Because extreme flood estimation is often constrained by relatively short instrumental records (typically under 50–200 years), **practitioners are increasingly turning to paleo-evidence and Indigenous knowledge to extend flood histories and improve flood frequency analysis** [11, 21]. 
*   **Hawkesbury River:** Aboriginal oral accounts have been utilized to identify a massive, highly destructive flood that occurred approximately 26 years prior to British settlement, significantly altering the understanding of rare flood frequencies in a valley known for having the highest flood risk exposure in Australia [21-23].
*   **Brisbane River:** Forensic examinations utilizing paleoclimate data, cosmogenic flood evidence, and Indigenous oral history suggest an extreme flood occurred in the 1700s that exceeded the largest recorded events of 1841 and 1893, demonstrating that evidence-based gathering techniques can effectively complement traditional flood estimation methods [24, 25].

**Innovations in Extreme Flood Modelling and Forecasting**
To better predict and manage these extreme events, researchers are introducing sophisticated modelling techniques across the industry:
*   **Ensemble and Probabilistic Forecasting:** There is a major transition from deterministic models to continuous ensemble forecasting. For instance, new operational systems for Hydro Tasmania and the Hawkesbury-Nepean Valley generate hundreds of ensemble forecast members to better quantify the likelihood and uncertainty of extreme inflows, providing critical lead time for emergency evacuations and dam spill management [26-29].
*   **Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning:** Researchers are integrating AI, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), to capture complex, non-linear relationships between large-scale climate indices (like ENSO and the Indian Ocean Dipole) and extreme streamflow variability [30-33].
*   **Joint Probability Assessments:** Extreme events often feature compounding hazards. Studies employ mixed methods (such as Monte Carlo analysis and the Design Variable Method) to understand the joint probability of extreme catchment flows coinciding with coastal storm tides or downstream tributary floods. This is critical for accurate dam failure consequence assessments and coastal urban planning [34, 35].
*   **Storm-Type Specific Analysis:** Analysts have recognized that different weather systems (e.g., East Coast Lows vs. convective thunderstorms) dominate extreme floods at varying durations and exceedance probabilities. This suggests that design inputs—like "pre-burst" rainfall and initial losses—should be tailored to specific storm types rather than broadly averaged, preventing the underestimation of extreme flood magnitudes [36-38].
