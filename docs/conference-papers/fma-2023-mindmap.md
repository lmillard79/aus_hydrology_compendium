## Practitioner Insights

*Key recommendations for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals.*

RESPONSE:
**1. Move Beyond Static Maps to Communicate Impact (The DIKW Model)**
*   **Recognize Mapping Limitations:** Maps are fundamental for showing constraints like flood extents, hazard (depth x velocity), and flood function, but they only tell part of the story [1, 2]. They must be supplemented with considerations for effective warning times, disruptions to community services (power, water), and antecedent catchment conditions [3, 4].
*   **Apply the DIKW (Data, Information, Knowledge, Wisdom) Pyramid:** Transition from presenting raw technical data to providing actionable wisdom [5]. The general public often struggles to interpret complex metrics like Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) or velocity [6]. 
*   **Contextualize the Data:** Instead of providing abstract velocity metrics (e.g., meters per second), contextualize the information by explaining the actual erosion risk to a property and outlining specific mitigation measures [6]. Use historical flood events that the community can relate to, rather than abstract design events, to communicate potential flood depths [6].

**2. Leverage Machine Learning and Real-Time Decision Support**
*   **Adopt Machine Learning (ML) for Rapid Forecasting:** Traditional 2D hydrodynamic models are highly accurate but often too slow for dynamic, real-time emergency response [7]. ML models can run in seconds (compared to hours for traditional models), enabling real-time forecasting, the rapid assessment of multiple rainfall scenarios (ensemble modeling), and continuous calibration using live data streams like social media or cameras [8-11].
*   **Implement Integrated Dashboards:** Establish automated, real-time dashboards to serve as a "single source of truth" during flood events. This prevents misinformation, aids in proactive disaster response, and saves critical time for emergency coordinators who previously had to manually draft status reports [12-14].
*   **Utilize 3D Visualizations and Augmented Reality (AR):** Use 3D hydrodynamic models and animations to vividly demonstrate the impacts of climate change and mitigation options to non-expert communities [15]. Emerging tools like AR overlays on Google Street View can help residents visually understand exactly how high floodwaters will reach on their specific property [16, 17].

**3. Optimize Warning Infrastructure and Data Collection**
*   **Audit and Upgrade Monitoring Networks:** Many flood monitoring networks developed organically for other purposes (e.g., water supply) and have critical gaps, such as unmonitored tributaries or rating curves that fail to capture extreme high-flow levels [18, 19]. Practitioners should comprehensively review and optimize their networks to ensure they are fit for flood forecasting [20].
*   **Ground Flood Classifications in Local Reality:** Ensure that flood classifications (minor, moderate, major) are explicitly linked to on-the-ground impacts by leveraging local intelligence and asset databases [21, 22]. This allows communities to translate a "14.2m gauge prediction" into specific, localized actions [23, 24].
*   **Harness Crowdsourced Data:** To address the scarcity of concurrent rainfall data in urban areas, incorporate data from crowdsourced Personal Weather Stations (PWS). While this data is uncontrolled, applying advanced signal filtering and quality control routines can unlock highly granular, valuable precipitation data to drastically improve the calibration of flood models [25-27].

**4. Plan for the "Unprecedented" with Actionable Emergency Management**
*   **Stop Using "Unprecedented" as an Excuse:** The term "unprecedented" is frequently used to excuse sub-optimal responses to events that were actually modeled or historically observed [28, 29]. Practitioners must proactively plan for events larger than the 1% AEP (1-in-100 year) event and past floods of record [15, 30].
*   **Design Scalable and Realistic Emergency Plans:** Flood emergency plans must be scalable, adaptable, and based on realistic human behavior [31, 32]. 
*   **Re-evaluate "Shelter-in-Place" Strategies:** The safest place during a flood is entirely away from the floodplain. "Shelter-in-place" strategies are highly risky and only viable if floodwaters rise and fall very quickly, and if essential services (sewerage, power, medical access) remain intact [33, 34]. 

**5. Implement Practical Mitigation: Levees and Nature-Based Solutions**
*   **Categorize and Manage Levees:** Unmanaged rural levees often fail during major floods. Practitioners should categorize levees by strategic importance (High, Significant, Not Significant) [35, 36]. For strategically important unmanaged levees, pre-determine and document the temporary reinforcement works required so they can be swiftly implemented in the days leading up to a flood [37].
*   **Integrate Nature-Based Solutions (NbS):** Shift toward holistic floodplain management by incorporating NbS, such as restoring wetlands and maintaining active floodplains. These solutions balance flood risk reduction with vital co-benefits, including biodiversity conservation, water quality improvement, and carbon sequestration [38-40].

## Cross-Cutting Connections

*How the research themes in this conference connect and interact.*

RESPONSE:
The research themes in this notebook are highly interconnected, reflecting a paradigm shift in floodplain management from a purely technical, engineering-based approach (creating static maps) to a dynamic, interdisciplinary approach focused on real-time emergency response and human vulnerability. 

Here is a comprehensive breakdown of how these cross-cutting themes connect, along with specific examples of papers that bridge multiple areas.

### Cross-Cutting Issues and Interdisciplinary Connections

**1. The Transition from "Mappable Constraints" to "Factors Beyond Maps"**
Traditional flood risk management has heavily relied on **Mappable Constraints** such as flood extents, depths, velocities, flow conveyance, and hazard classifications [1], [2]. However, a major cross-cutting issue is the recognition of **Flood Mapping Limitations**. Static maps only tell part of the story of flood risk and cannot be used in isolation to inform decisions [3]. They fail to capture critical **Factors Beyond Maps**, such as the physical condition of mitigation infrastructure, disruptions to community services (power, communications), and the vulnerability of different demographic groups [4], [5], [6]. This connects urban planning and hydraulic engineering with sociology and emergency management, emphasizing that understanding human behavior and situational context is just as important as understanding water flow [4], [7].

**2. "Unprecedented" Events Expose Weaknesses in "Warning Infrastructure"**
The theme of **Unprecedented vs Unplanned** directly intersects with **Warning Infrastructure** and **Flood Mapping Limitations**. During the 2022 floods, water levels frequently exceeded historical records, meaning events went "beyond the rating curve" of existing stream gauges [8], [9], [10]. Because the events exceeded previous datasets, existing maps and warning systems were inadequate, leading to delayed or inaccurate warnings [8], [9]. The literature argues that while the weather itself may be unprecedented, the *disaster* is often a result of being "unplanned"—meaning our planning and response systems lacked the capability to scale and adapt to unpredictable hazards [11], [12], [13]. 

**3. "Best Practice Solutions" Bridge the Gap Between Static Maps and Real-Time Warnings**
To address the limitations of traditional maps and the unpredictability of "unprecedented" floods, **Best Practice Solutions** increasingly rely on technology to modernize **Warning Infrastructure**. Interdisciplinary solutions combining data science, machine learning, and disaster management are being used to create dynamic, real-time flood intelligence [14], [15]. For example, shifting from static PDF maps to interactive, automated predictive dashboards allows emergency responders to understand exact impacts—such as which roads will be cut off and when—turning reactive emergency responses into proactive, preventative actions [16], [17], [18].

### Specific Examples of Papers Bridging Multiple Themes

**1. *Managing Flooding Beyond Maps* by A. Toniato & D. McLuckie (Department of Planning and Environment)**
*   **Themes Bridged:** Flood Mapping Limitations, Mappable Constraints, Factors Beyond Maps.
*   **Connection:** This paper explicitly bridges the gap between hydraulic engineering and holistic risk management. The authors explain that while we can map constraints like flood function and hazard, maps are only an "indicator of impacts" [3], [1], [19]. To effectively manage risk, decision-makers must look at factors beyond the map, such as effective warning time, community readiness, evacuation problems, and disruptions to vital community services [4], [20], [5]. The paper highlights that managing flood risk requires technical advice and understanding of natural variability that a static drawing simply cannot provide [6], [21], [22].

**2. *Unprecedented or Unplanned* by N. Bichel (FloodMapp)**
*   **Themes Bridged:** Unprecedented vs Unplanned, Flood Mapping Limitations, Best Practice Solutions.
*   **Connection:** Bichel challenges the frequent use of the word "unprecedented" to describe the 2022 floods, arguing that it is often used to excuse a sub-optimal, reactive emergency response [23], [24]. The paper bridges this concept to **Best Practice Solutions** by showcasing how Transport for NSW utilized FloodMapp's real-time, dynamic flood intelligence dashboard [16], [18]. Because static flood maps lacked the scale and adaptability needed for a rapidly evolving event, the implementation of dynamic situational awareness tools allowed emergency managers to actively manage road closures, predict isolation, and optimize logistics in real-time—proving that severe impacts can be mitigated through better planning and technology [18], [25], [26]. 

**3. *Beyond the Rating Curve – Forecasting the 'Unprecedented' Flood* by A. Clifton et al. (Bureau of Meteorology & NSW SES)**
*   **Themes Bridged:** Warning Infrastructure, Unprecedented vs Unplanned, Best Practice Solutions.
*   **Connection:** This paper focuses on the failure and subsequent adaptation of **Warning Infrastructure** during the 2022 "unprecedented" floods [10]. It highlights the deep uncertainty that occurs when floods exceed the 1% Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) and rise above historical gauge heights, rendering existing historical data and rating curves unreliable [9], [10]. As a **Best Practice Solution**, the paper discusses how the Bureau of Meteorology collaborated in real-time with emergency agencies to use alternative spatial tools (like MODIS satellite imagery) and recession forecasting to optimize warnings and direct rescue assets when traditional infrastructure was overwhelmed [27], [10].

**4. *Community Flood Intelligence Portals and Automated Flood Forecasting* by D. Danielsson (Toowoomba Regional Council)**
*   **Themes Bridged:** Warning Infrastructure, Best Practice Solutions, Factors Beyond Maps.
*   **Connection:** This paper connects the physical installation of warning gauges to community behavior. Despite investing $1.5M in gauge infrastructure, Toowoomba realized the public was largely unaware of it because raw gauge data lacks context [17]. To solve this, they developed a **Best Practice Solution**: a public-facing disaster dashboard that translates raw river heights into location-specific, actionable intelligence for the community [17], [28]. By integrating real-time warning infrastructure with an easy-to-understand portal, the council successfully addressed the human element (factors beyond maps), ensuring that vulnerable populations could proactively understand their individual risk and make informed evacuation decisions [17], [29]. 

**5. *A Thousand Gauges: Using Personal Weather Stations to Hindcast Extreme Flood Events* by J. Chorley & E. O’Brien (SMEC)**
*   **Themes Bridged:** Warning Infrastructure, Flood Mapping Limitations, Best Practice Solutions.
*   **Connection:** This paper identifies a critical limitation in flood mapping and forecasting: the "garbage in, garbage out" problem caused by sparse official rainfall gauges in urban areas [30], [31]. As a novel solution to this **Warning Infrastructure** deficit, the authors utilized crowdsourced data from over 1,000 personal weather stations via the IBM weather network [30], [31]. By using advanced signal filtering and machine learning on this uncontrolled data, they were able to dramatically improve the predictive skill of urban drainage models, bridging the gap between data scarcity and highly accurate flood forecasting [32], [31].

## Research Gaps

*Unanswered questions and future directions identified in the literature.*

RESPONSE:
The provided papers identify numerous research gaps, unanswered questions, and future directions across the spectrum of flood risk management. These gaps span data collection, predictive modeling, emergency planning, and community communication.

**Data and Monitoring Gaps**
*   **Historical and Paleo Data:** The concept of "unprecedented" floods is currently limited by the timeframe of European settlement. There is a significant opportunity and future direction to formally incorporate under-utilised flood information from Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people’s records, as well as paleo flood data, into modern flood intelligence [1].
*   **Data Sparsity in Extreme Events:** There is little to no historical data to validate models for low Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) floods (1% or rarer) [2]. Catchments often suffer from heterogeneity in rainfall coverage, unmonitored tributaries, and a lack of reliable rating curves that extend to high-flow levels [3-5]. 
*   **Post-Flood Data Sharing:** The sharing of post-flood data between organisations and industry professionals is currently considered "immature" [6]. Establishing better ways to pool flood information is a necessary area of improvement to ensure more accurate and consistent flood mapping [6].
*   **Personal Weather Stations (PWS):** While crowdsourced PWS data offers a way to fill rainfall data gaps, future research should focus on routing the confidence intervals of PWS data through flood models to perform more rigorous uncertainty analyses [7, 8].

**Modeling and Technology Gaps**
*   **Machine Learning (ML) vs. Traditional Models:** As the industry explores AI and ML for real-time forecasting, several unanswered questions remain: *What degree of accuracy is sufficient for flood risk warnings? At what point do model run-time requirements make traditional systems inadequate? How do traditional model development costs compare to machine learning development costs?* [9]. Future directions include integrating ML models with new data streams, such as CCTV imaging, maintenance requests, and social media [10, 11]. 
*   **Uncertainty Analysis:** There is a poor understanding of how to evaluate modeling uncertainty. Key barriers include a lack of supporting guidance, training, and suitable digital tools, making the consistent application of uncertainty analysis a critical future direction [12].
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Future research needs to prioritize sensitivity analyses to identify which parameters (e.g., Manning's roughness, bridge losses) matter most in complex flood models [13].

**Gaps in Nature-Based Solutions (NbS)**
*   **Empirical Data Shortages:** There is very little empirical data on the impact of NbS on flooding, with existing data mostly restricted to "small" floods [14]. 
*   **Vegetation Impacts:** More research is needed to understand the extent to which different vegetation types impact drag force, roughness, and changing water velocity during floods [15].
*   **System-Wide Co-benefits:** There is a lack of empirical data on how the ecological co-benefits of NbS function collectively as a whole system, highlighting an area needing deeper investigation [15].

**Emergency Management, Planning, and Policy Gaps**
*   **Shelter-in-Place and Isolation:** The safety of shelter-in-place strategies remains an unanswered question in many contexts. A key research gap is defining "how quick is quickly" when relying on floodwaters to recede. The papers note that effort must be directed toward investigating the tolerable duration of isolation for communities with different vulnerabilities, emphasizing that this analysis must incorporate social science alongside flood hazard considerations [16, 17].
*   **Flood Emergency Management Plans (FEMPs):** More work is needed to understand when it is actually appropriate to use FEMPs to manage residual risk for future developments, as they often rely on naive assumptions about human behavior [18].
*   **Unmanaged Levees:** A potential gap in current management frameworks is the lack of mechanisms for reinforcing unmanaged levees that provide a strategic benefit ahead of a flood [19].
*   **Insurance Definitions:** The distinction between "stormwater" and "flooding" remains a highly contentious issue. Future discussions and policy developments are needed to reclassify the definition of flooding to streamline the insurance process and reduce the mental toll on insured residents [20].
*   **Updating National Guidelines:** Keeping foundational documents like Australian Rainfall and Runoff (AR&R) relevant and up-to-date in a rapidly changing climate is an ongoing community issue that requires strong advocacy and resolution [21]. 

**Communication and Visualisation Gaps**
*   **Visualising Exceedances:** A future direction for flood mapping is figuring out more useful, intuitive ways to show flood impact objective exceedances to the public and policymakers [22, 23]. 
*   **Augmented Reality (AR):** The future of flood applications includes integrating AR to overlay specified flood levels onto tools like Google Street View, providing a more realistic and understandable depiction of potential flood depths against properties for the general public [24].
