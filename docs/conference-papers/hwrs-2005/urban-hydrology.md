## Detailed Analysis

**The transition to Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD):**
Traditional urban drainage historically focused on efficiently conveying stormwater away from developed areas to receiving waters [1]. However, the symposium highlights a major shift toward Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD), which integrates water cycle management into urban planning to protect natural systems, reduce runoff and peak flows, and improve water quality [1]. **Pervious pavements are highlighted as a key WSUD component capable of reducing flood peaks and trapping pollutants—such as sediments, phosphorus, heavy metals, and hydrocarbons—at the source before they reach receiving waters** [2-5]. Constructed wetlands are also extensively analysed for treating urban runoff, particularly for managing nitrogen [6, 7]. **Research on Melbourne's stormwater indicates that nitrogen in urban runoff is predominantly dissolved (around 77%), meaning wetlands must be specifically designed to treat dissolved nitrogen under highly variable, stochastic flow conditions** [6, 8]. 

**Advanced Modelling and Triple-Bottom-Line Assessments:**
To evaluate urban stormwater systems, the **Model for Urban Stormwater Improvement Conceptualisation (MUSIC)** is heavily utilised to assess conceptual designs and meet water quality objectives [9]. MUSIC has been enhanced with a life-cycle costing module to facilitate **Triple-Bottom-Line (TBL) assessments, integrating financial, social, and ecological considerations into urban stormwater projects** [10, 11]. This allows urban water managers to move away from traditional, isolated decision-making toward more systematic, participatory, and cost-effective planning [12, 13]. Other models like the **US EPA Storm Water Management Model (SWMM)** are used to simulate complex hydrologic and hydraulic problems in urban drainage networks, such as Sydney's Powells Creek [14]. Additionally, researchers are refining how **Areal Reduction Factors (ARFs)** are applied in urban catchments for design flood estimation, testing new spatial distributions to overcome flow continuity problems in hydraulic models [15, 16]. 

**Education and Global Urban Pressures:**
Recognising the rapid evolution of urban hydrology, educational tools like the **Electronic Stormwater Management Education package (ESME)** have been developed to contrast past flood-mitigation approaches with modern WSUD [17, 18]. Furthermore, Catchment Modelling Toolkit products are being integrated into university curricula so that new engineering graduates can expertly design sophisticated, integrated urban water management strategies [19-21]. Finally, the symposium addresses the broader global impacts of uncontrolled urbanisation on water resources, noting that **rapid urban growth—such as in Ho Chi Minh City—can severely degrade water supply infrastructure, increase illegal groundwater abstraction, and introduce serious health risks due to unmet water demands** [22-24].
