## Detailed Analysis

Research from the 10th Hydraulics Conference investigates **flow resistance** through the lens of partitioned boundary stresses, vegetation, and predictive equations, while also addressing **bedrock evolution** in steep mountain rivers through new stream power modeling techniques.

**Flow Resistance**
The conference papers analyze flow resistance across diverse hydraulic environments, emphasizing the need to break down total resistance into its constituent physical forces:
*   **Skin Friction and Form Drag:** Total flow resistance in fixed and mobile boundaries is widely evaluated by partitioning it into **skin friction** and **form drag** [1]. Researchers re-examined Einstein’s principle of the "summation of boundary shear stress" to explicitly quantify the relationship between form drag and the length or volume of flow separation zones that form behind wavy roughness elements, such as sand dunes [1-3]. 
*   **Configuration vs. Grain Resistance:** In the context of flash floods surging down stepped waterways, resistance is similarly partitioned into **configuration resistance** (driven by the flow accelerating over step edges and decelerating upon collision with downstream step faces) and **grain resistance** (the shear stress generated by the physical sand or bed grains) [4]. A rougher stepped bed significantly postpones flash flood propagation and enhances energy dissipation [5].
*   **Macro-Roughness and Vegetation:** The presence of macro-roughness elements—like large, immobile boulders—disrupts flow and bears a large portion of the total shear stress by increasing form drag, which in turn significantly reduces the remaining shear stress available to entrain mobile sediments [6, 7]. Furthermore, numerous studies in the proceedings dedicated efforts to quantifying the drag, turbulence, and flow resistance induced by varying types of vegetation, such as emergent canopies, flexible foliated plant stands, and woody riparian vegetation [8-13].
*   **Hydraulic Bores:** For hydraulic bores propagating in rough-bed open channels, bottom frictional resistance is a critical force [14]. Using momentum conservation laws, researchers demonstrated that bottom friction forces and gravity rival the pressure differences at the bore head [15, 16]. Models show that the friction coefficient of a bore converges toward standard steady-state open-channel values as the slope increases or as the water level difference around the bore head decreases [17].
*   **Predictive Equations in Gravel-Bed Rivers:** When predicting resistance in natural gravel and boulder-bed rivers, researchers compared classic logarithmic equations (based on the law of the wall) against $q$-based (discharge-based) equations using a dataset of 3,318 field measurements [18]. They found that logarithmic equations relying on the hydraulic radius perform poorly because the radius is difficult to measure accurately in irregular sections, often amplifying calculation errors [19]. Conversely, **$q$-based equations** (such as the Smart & Jaeggi or Ferguson equations) do not require iterative site calibration and provide highly efficient, generalized velocity predictions across a vast range of channel slopes, widths, and grain diameters [18, 19].

**Bedrock Evolution**
Mountain rivers are characterized by steep slopes and severe bed changes that frequently flush away the protective sediment armor layer. This exposes the underlying bedrock, subsequently accelerating **channel incision** and erosion [20].
*   **Incision Mechanisms and Erodibility:** The hydraulic erodibility of bedrock is evaluated by relating the **rate of energy dissipation** of the flowing water to the inherent erodibility of the geological material [21]. To quantify this, researchers introduced a novel **stream power bedrock incision rate formula** [20]. This formula dictates that the bedrock erosion rate is a function of the flow's depth-averaged velocity and the excess stream power (the actual stream power minus a **critical stream power** threshold) [22]. The critical stream power explicitly accounts for the specific erodibility coefficient of the bedrock [22].
*   **Modeling Bedrock Morphodynamics:** To simulate these severe morphodynamic changes, researchers proposed a two-dimensional mobile-bed model known as the **Explicit Finite Analytic (EFA) model** [20]. A key advantage of the EFA modeling concept is its ability to simultaneously account for the rapid erosion of soft bedrock and the protective "cover effect" provided when sediment deposits over the bedrock layer [20]. Field data collected over three years at the Taan River in Taiwan verified that this model is highly capable of simulating morphological evolution in real-world bedrock reaches [20, 22].
