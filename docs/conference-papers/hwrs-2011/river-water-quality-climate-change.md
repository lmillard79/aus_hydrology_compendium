## Detailed Analysis

Research presented at the 10th Hydraulics Conference indicates that climate change—specifically through increased average air temperatures and shifting precipitation patterns—has profound, detrimental impacts on river and surface water quality [1, 2]. 

**Direct Impacts on Water Quality Indicators**
The most immediate reactions to climate change in river systems are altered streamflows and elevated water temperatures [2]. Using models like QUAL2K to simulate hypothetical climate change scenarios (such as decreased flow and increased temperature), researchers found a **significant decrease in Dissolved Oxygen (DO) levels alongside an increase in Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD) and overall water temperature** [3-5]. Higher water temperatures accelerate chemical and bacteriological processes within the river, thereby increasing algal growth, decay, and respiration rates [2, 6]. 

**Even in scenarios where streamflow remains completely unchanged, an increase in air temperature of just 1°C can cause DO levels to decrease**, indicating that climate change impacts on DO can be driven entirely by water warming [7]. Furthermore, when climate change causes reduced river flows, **pollutants become more concentrated within the water column**, which exacerbates water quality degradation and increases the likelihood of violating safe water quality standards [5].

**Broader Catchment and Reservoir Effects**
Climate change also degrades water quality through altered runoff and reservoir dynamics. In areas projected to experience increased precipitation, **higher surface runoff transports more pathogens from the catchment into water sources and increases the frequency of sewer overflows**, posing elevated risks to drinking water supplies [8]. 

Conversely, regions experiencing decreased precipitation and increased evapotranspiration face severe droughts and large water level fluctuations in their reservoirs [9, 10]. These large fluctuations and shifting atmospheric forces can cause **temperature increases in bottom waters, which stimulate bacterial activity, increase sediment oxygen demand (SOD), and ultimately deplete DO in the hypolimnion** [11].

**The Need for New Management and Adaptation Policies**
Because the increase in water temperature and the decrease in river flows are naturally occurring and not directly controllable, environmental engineers and policymakers must focus on long-term adaptation strategies to manage water quality [12, 13]. Studies applying decision-support tools like the Fuzzy Waste Load Allocation Model (FWLAM) demonstrate that **current water quality standards and fractional removal policies for effluents will be heavily inadequate under future climate conditions** [3, 14, 15]. For example, to maintain acceptable water quality in the face of warming and lower flows, the required **fractional removal levels of pollutants at treatment plants may need to increase drastically from a current average of about 35% up to 90%** [14].

To mitigate these undesirable impacts on water quality, recommended adaptation policies include **mandating minimum instream environmental flows to help dilute pollutant loadings and lower river temperatures, upgrading municipal and industrial wastewater treatment plants, and rigorously revising the allowable discharge standards set by pollution control agencies** [13, 14].
