## Detailed Analysis

Within the broader research presented at the 10th Hydraulics Conference, investigations into localized scour downstream of 2-D wall jets highlight a complex, unstable phenomenon known as **jet-flipping**, which prevents the sediment bed from ever reaching a stable equilibrium scour state [1, 2]. 

This phenomenon is characterized by an **intermittent and cyclical action where the flow of the jet suddenly flips its trajectory from the channel bed to the water surface and vice versa** [1, 2]. This creates two distinct hydrodynamic phases that govern sediment transport and scour morphology:

*   **The Digging Phase (Bed-Jet):** The jet acts as an attached bed-jet that vigorously scours the sediment, forming a deep scour hole characterized by a distinct downstream ridge [1, 3]. During this phase, the water surface remains relatively calm, and the **maximum scour depth is achieved, which can be up to 37% deeper than the scour depth observed during the filling phase** [1, 4].
*   **The Filling Phase (Surface-Jet):** When the jet flips towards the water surface, it transforms into a surface-jet [1, 3]. This surface flow causes sand particles located on the previously formed downstream ridge to roll back down the slope and re-fill the scour hole [3]. As a result, the bed profile lengthens downstream, the ridge flattens and lowers in height, and the scour depth reaches its cyclical minimum [3].

The temporal dynamics of these two phases shift significantly as the scouring progresses over time. **The filling phase is consistently much longer than the digging phase** [1, 5, 6]. Furthermore, over multiple cycles, the duration of the digging phase remains relatively constant, but **the filling phase takes increasingly longer to complete** [1, 6]. This progressive lengthening occurs because sediment material is gradually lost from the downstream ridge with each successive jet-flipping cycle, requiring more time for the surface-jet to displace enough sediment to complete the filling action [1, 6].

The occurrence and frequency of jet-flipping are heavily dictated by specific structural and hydraulic conditions, most notably the **apron length and the jet submergence ratio** [2, 7]. Experimental results indicate that the **average time required to complete one full digging-filling cycle increases exponentially as the length of the apron decreases** [6, 8]. The presence of an apron plays a critical role in triggering this instability; in test runs where no apron was present, jet-flipping did not occur at all, and the jet simply dug continuously into the sediment bed to a maximum depth [6, 8].
