Effective governance and programmatic structures are critical to managing the complex, overlapping challenges of floodplain management and disaster adaptation. The sources indicate a necessary transition away from fragmented, reactive, and top-down decision-making toward **collaborative, multi-hazard, and proactive frameworks** that integrate land-use planning, community engagement, and long-term investment.

## State and Regional Governance Programs

Various Australian states are implementing comprehensive programs to guide local and regional floodplain management:

- **New South Wales (NSW):** The State operates the long-standing Floodplain Management Program, which partners with local councils to provide financial support and technical guidance for developing Flood Risk Management Plans (FRMPs) [1-3]. Building on this, the NSW Reconstruction Authority is developing **Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs)** [4, 5]. DAPs are the state's first multi-hazard regional plans, designed to understand compound risks—such as floods, coastal hazards, bushfires, and landslides—and guide long-term investment in risk reduction [4, 6, 7]. DAPs are locally shaped, regionally coordinated, and state-facilitated, operating through collaborative governance structures like Regional Steering Committees that bring together all levels of government [4, 7-9].
- **Queensland:** The Queensland Reconstruction Authority (QRA) manages the $49 million Flood Risk Management Program (FRMP), which provides a consistent technical foundation for 39 local governments to undertake evidence-based hazard mitigation [10, 11]. Recognizing that disaster funding has historically been reactive and event-based, Queensland is **shifting toward a proactive 10-year flood risk management investment plan** to streamline funding and guide long-term disaster risk reduction [12, 13].
- **Victoria:** Melbourne Water exemplifies collaborative governance by partnering with 38 local government agencies to co-deliver a regional flood modelling program [14-16]. This establishes a shared responsibility for flood and drainage management, utilizing joint Project Control Groups and standardized funding agreements [14, 17, 18].

## Systemic Challenges in Governance

Despite these programs, several structural barriers hinder effective disaster adaptation:

- **Fragmented Accountability and Top-Down Cultures:** Decision-making responsibilities that dictate flood risk are often disjointed [19]. Historically, the sector has relied on "deficit-based" or technocratic, expert-led approaches that fail to foster genuine community participation, locking communities into rigid structural pathways [19, 20]. At the municipal level, overlapping boundaries and dispersed accountability lead to backlogs in implementing vital flood studies [21].
- **Capacity and Resourcing Constraints:** Local governments possess varying degrees of financial resources, technical knowledge, and personnel [11]. In programs like the NSW FRMP and Coastal Management Framework, local funding envelopes are often insufficient to implement all identified mitigation measures, highlighting the need for higher-level adaptation plans to unlock alternative funding [22-24].
- **Friction with Land-Use Planning:** There is a recognized disconnect between flood modelling and statutory urban planning [25, 26]. While legislation requires councils to plan for climate change and apply flood overlays, planning scheme amendments are frequently derailed by public unrest or resistance [27, 28]. Addressing the compounding crises of housing affordability and climate change requires deep collaboration between disparate agencies responsible for disaster planning and housing policy [25, 29].

## Pathways to Improved Adaptation Governance

To overcome these barriers, the sources recommend several governance strategies:

- **Inclusive, Community-Led Co-Design:** Programs like *Flood to Flourish* emphasize placing communities, Traditional Owners, emergency services, and local governments on an equal footing [19, 30, 31]. Meaningful disaster adaptation requires strong relational foundations, integrating local and Indigenous cultural values into formal risk management frameworks [32-35].
- **"One Government" Approaches:** To reduce engagement fatigue and improve strategic alignment, agencies must coordinate closely [9]. Implementing streamlined, cross-agency guidance, standardized toolkits, and clear interagency agreements can resolve ambiguities regarding emergency operations and long-term planning [34, 36].
- **Structured Investment Frameworks:** Drawing inspiration from international models like the UK's partnership funding and "Six Capitals" approach, Australian governance must facilitate long-term, cross-boundary investment [37-40]. This ensures that funding prioritizes sustainable, nature-based, and socially beneficial resilience measures rather than just reacting in the aftermath of disasters [12, 38, 41].
