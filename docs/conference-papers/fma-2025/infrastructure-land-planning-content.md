**The fundamental challenge of floodplain management is that retrospective flood protection is difficult and expensive, making it critical to safely locate new developments and embed resilience at the land planning stage** [1, 2]. As climate change worsens flood hazards and urban areas expand, the traditional approach of simply applying flood overlays at the permit stage is becoming strained, requiring a more integrated, proactive approach to land use and infrastructure [1-3].

**Transitioning to Risk-Based and Hazard-Based Land Use Planning**
The sources emphasize a shift away from static assessments toward risk-based and hazard-based planning frameworks:
*   **Proportional and Tolerable Risk:** In New South Wales, updated planning circulars (e.g., Circular 24-001) require that planning decisions balance the merits, risks, and impacts of a proposed development [4]. The goal is to limit flood impacts to an acceptable level and achieve a "tolerable" flood risk profile that factors in events up to the probable maximum flood [5-7].
*   **Hazard-Based Controls:** Melbourne Water is advancing a hazard-based approach that applies the strictest development restrictions to high-hazard areas to protect the natural conveyance and storage functions of floodplains [8, 9]. Conversely, in low-hazard areas, planning processes can be streamlined to support land use changes, provided that site safety and access are not compromised and downstream impacts are avoided [10].

**Regional and Multi-Hazard Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs)**
To manage disaster risk systemically, planning is moving beyond isolated local studies to regional, multi-hazard strategies. 
*   **Adaptation Pathways:** The NSW Reconstruction Authority is developing Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs) for highly vulnerable regions like the Northern Rivers and the Hawkesbury-Nepean Valley [11, 12]. These plans assess the compounding impacts of multiple natural hazards (floods, coastal hazards, bushfires, landslides) to prioritize and stage regional-scale investments in mitigation infrastructure [13-15]. 
*   **Unlocking Investment:** By consolidating local flood risk management plans into a regional framework, DAPs establish shared adaptation pathways that guide when and how governments should invest in large-scale hazard risk reduction [16-18].

**Adaptive Infrastructure and Cascading Hazards**
In environments facing complex, cascading hazards, infrastructure planning requires dynamic tools:
*   **Project Response Indicators (PRIs):** A multi-hazard study in Christchurch, New Zealand, evaluated coincident flooding (pluvial, fluvial, coastal, and groundwater) exacerbated by earthquake-induced vertical land movement [19-21]. To manage this, planners developed "Project Response Indicators" (PRIs) [19, 22]. A PRI is a quantitative metric that defines the threshold at which a specific element in the floodplain (such as a residential building, road, or wastewater pump station) becomes unacceptably vulnerable, triggering the need for a targeted management or infrastructure response [23-25].
*   **Tension Between Planning and Adaptation:** A major challenge in infrastructure planning is the tension between the "precautionary approach" (making a single, permanent intervention at the start of a building's life, like setting a floor height) and the "managed adaptive approach" (which involves continuous interventions and flexible changes over time as climate data evolves) [26].

**Integration with Emergency Management**
Finally, infrastructure and land planning must directly support emergency operations. This includes mapping safe evacuation routes, integrating flood inundation data to auto-tag high-ground schools and hospitals as relief shelters [37, 38], and ensuring high-risk developments like caravan parks have robust Flood Emergency Response Plans [39]. Additionally, new guidelines help councils determine when designing for "shelter in place" is an appropriate land use strategy for developments trapped in flash-flood environments where evacuation is impossible [39, 40].
