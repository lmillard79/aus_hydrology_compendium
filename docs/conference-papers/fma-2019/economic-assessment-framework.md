## Detailed Analysis

In Floodplain Risk Management (FRM), an economic assessment is a fundamental step used to evaluate, prioritize, and select flood mitigation options [1, 2]. FRM options should be tested against current management practices and existing community exposure, which requires a thorough understanding of the social, economic, and environmental benefits and costs of each option [3, 4]. 

**Current Challenges in Economic Assessment**
Historically, the economic assessment of FRM projects—typically conducted through a Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA) combined with a Multi-Criteria Assessment (MCA)—has focused primarily on quantifying direct property-related flood damages [2, 5]. This narrow focus presents several challenges:
*   **Underestimation of Benefits:** By mostly accounting for property damage, CBA results for FRM options are often highly conservative and can underestimate the overall economic benefit, leading to low Benefit-Cost Ratios (BCR) [2, 5, 6].
*   **Funding Disparity:** This conservative approach creates a disparity when comparing flood mitigation projects to other major government infrastructure projects, which often use more holistic economic analyses that capture a wider range of benefits [5-7].
*   **Cost vs. Effort Imbalance:** While it is desirable to quantify other benefits (like environmental improvements, risk to life, and intangible social impacts such as mental health, amenity, and safety), doing so requires significantly greater effort and reliable data [6, 8, 9]. Because many FRM capital projects cost less than $1 million, it is often difficult to justify the expense of a highly detailed, holistic CBA [6]. 

**The Draft Economic Assessment Framework**
To address these challenges, researchers and government bodies in NSW have developed a Draft Assessment Framework that ensures the time and resources spent on economic appraisal are proportional to the project's capital cost and influence on decision-making [7, 10]. The framework categorizes economic assessment into different levels based on project value, aligning with processes like the NSW Treasury Gateway Review [11-13]:

*   **Level 1 Assessment:** This is a strategic-level assessment typically undertaken during the initial FRM Study and Plan phase. It relies on relatively simple economic assessments, combining MCA and BCA focused on direct property damages [7, 13]. For low-value projects implementing works under $1 million, no further detailed economic assessment is required beyond a brief internal review of the Level 1 assumptions [12, 13].
*   **Level 2 Assessment (Projects $1M - $5M):** As projects move into the investigation and design phase, the Level 1 analysis is updated with more accurate cost estimates. Crucially, this level introduces "rapid assessment techniques" to estimate the monetary value of non-property damages (e.g., intangible impacts and risk to life) if those factors are likely to influence the project's viability [12, 13].
*   **Level 3 Assessment (Projects > $5M):** For larger projects, the assessment builds on Level 2 but utilizes more detailed techniques for monetary valuation [12, 13]. This may involve producing a standalone economic report or business case to support grant applications [13, 14]. Projects exceeding $10 million are generally subjected to the stringent, independent NSW Treasury Gateway Review process [11, 13].

Ultimately, this tiered framework aims to bridge the gap between the simple property-damage models traditionally used in FRM and the complex, holistic economic appraisals required for major infrastructure, ensuring that flood projects can be fairly evaluated and funded without demanding unjustifiable assessment costs [7, 15].
