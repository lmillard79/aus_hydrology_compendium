## Detailed Analysis

**The "Big Data" Challenge in FRM**
Modern Floodplain Risk Management (FRM) relies heavily on detailed flood models, which generate ever-increasing volumes of data [1, 2]. For example, the Sunshine Coast Regional Council manages over 10 terabytes of flooding data across multiple catchments [3]. Managing these large datasets in an ad-hoc manner presents a significant corporate risk [4, 5]. To effectively leverage this information, **best practice requires comprehensive data management systems that utilize metadata, master grids, and automatic archiving** [6-9]. This ensures that councils maintain an auditable "time history" of flood data, which is crucial for rolling back to historical data points to defend past planning decisions during legal challenges [10, 11].

**Raw vs. Quality-Controlled Data**
During extreme events like Cyclone Debbie, there is often high demand for near real-time "raw" data to support emergency response [12, 13]. However, **there can be significant differences between raw data and quality-controlled data** [14]. Raw data can be skewed by sensor technology limitations, localized hydraulic effects, or damage to the monitoring station [15]. Modellers face political and public pressure to produce models quickly after a disaster, which can lead them to use raw data or attempt their own quality control [16]. The sources emphasize that **the organization collecting the data is best placed to undertake quality control and coding**, and that the limitations of raw data must be thoroughly understood before it is used as a foundation for long-term modelling and decision-making [13, 16-18]. 

**Calibration, Verification, and Peer Review**
All flood models are approximations of reality, and quality assurance requires rigorous calibration to observed flood levels and streamflow gauges to reduce uncertainty [19]. The best modellers understand these limitations and **verify their models using independent checks, such as comparing the data to photographs and anecdotal evidence** [19, 20]. Additionally, **peer review of hydraulic models and mapping is considered essential** [21]. Subjecting data to internal or external peer review before release improves its accuracy and makes the information much more defensible when faced with community skepticism [22]. 

**Standardization and Information Sharing**
To maintain quality assurance at a broader level, studies often must meet minimum standards for data quality guided by state manuals and scrutinized by flood specialists [23]. In New South Wales, the use of the **NSW Flood Data Portal is required for all studies completed with state funding** [24]. This ensures that high-quality data is consistently handed over, stored, and shared between local councils, the Office of Environment and Heritage (OEH), and the State Emergency Service (SES) [24, 25]. 

**Managing Uncertainty in Data**
Understanding the specific accuracy limits of data is vital for FRM. For example, the industry is increasingly using algorithmic or remote survey capture for property floor levels; however, **practitioners must statistically assess what level of accuracy is actually required so they do not overinvest in data collection** without understanding its real impact on calculating flood damages [26]. Because unavoidable uncertainties in data and modelling will always persist, FRM relies on mechanisms like "freeboard"—a factor of safety added to the defined flood event—to account for these variables [27].
