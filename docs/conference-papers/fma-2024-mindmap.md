## Practitioner Insights

*Key recommendations for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals.*

RESPONSE:
**Incorporate Infragravity Waves in Coastal Creek Assessments**
*   **Update storm tide studies to explicitly consider infragravity (IG) waves.** Standard storm tide studies typically ignore IG waves (or "surf beat"), but recent observations in small coastal creeks (such as Intermittently Closed and Open Lakes and Lagoons, or ICOLLs) show that IG waves can cause peak water levels to exceed design storm tide values by as much as 550mm [1-3]. 
*   **Install low-cost, high-frequency water level gauges.** Traditional gauges, like those in the Bureau of Meteorology's network, do not adequately cover smaller coastal creeks [4]. Installing local, low-cost gauges with short recording intervals (e.g., 1 minute) is critical, as standard 10-minute intervals often miss individual wave crests and underestimate peak water levels [4, 5]. These gauges not only inform design levels but also provide real-time intelligence for flood warnings and ICOLL management [3].
*   **Supplement gauge data with alternative observation methods.** When evaluating small coastal creeks, incorporate field surveys and video footage, as video recordings have proven highly effective in identifying the progression of short wave crests and surging water levels associated with IG waves [4, 5].

**Optimize Economic Analysis using the 2023 DT01 Flood Damage Tool**
*   **Reassess past flood mitigation options using the updated tool.** The 2023 DT01 tool calculates commercial and industrial damages based on unit floor area ($/m2) rather than on a per-property basis [6, 7]. Benchmarking shows this leads to significantly higher damage estimates: residential damages average 73% higher, and non-residential damages can be 161% to over 261% higher compared to older tools [8-10]. Options that previously suffered from low Benefit-Cost Ratios (BCRs) should be re-evaluated, as the higher damage estimates may now prove them economically viable [11].
*   **Manually verify and clean building footprint data.** Because the 2023 tool calculates damages on a strict per-building basis using floor area, practitioners must meticulously clean spatial data (like Geoscape footprints) by removing secondary buildings and verifying assigned property uses prior to analysis [12].

**Transform Emergency Resources and Community Warnings**
*   **Shift from generic messaging to highly localized, scenario-based warnings.** Generic advice like "Know Your Flood Risk" is often poorly understood by the public [13, 14]. Warnings must be tailored to specific communities using relevant local language, empirical inputs, and historical context rather than relying solely on technical maps [15, 16].
*   **Develop Community-Based Flood Action Plans with clear triggers.** Create action plans that outline a set of specific, triggerable events (such as the exceedance of a specific upstream river gauge) that clearly tell the community when to prepare and respond [17-19]. 
*   **Engage local community contacts in plan development.** Ensure that key community members review the action plans to grant them local credibility and ensure the proposed actions are practically achievable during a crisis [20].

**Apply Actionable Insights for Hydrology and Future Planning**
*   **Account for restricted "drainage windows" due to Sea Level Rise.** Do not assume coastal floodplains can continuously drain. Water can only discharge from the floodplain when internal water levels are higher than the tide [21, 22]. As sea levels rise, the window of time available for gravity drainage will dramatically shrink, leading to chronic waterlogging. Future land-use planning must account for this loss of function [21, 23].
*   **Adopt area-based damage assessments for agricultural land.** When conducting cost-benefit analyses in rural areas, use tools that assess damages on an "area" basis rather than a "point" basis to accurately quantify crop losses and damages to outside assets (like farm infrastructure), which are significantly underestimated by traditional methods [24].
*   **Validate models to design better monitoring infrastructure.** Use empirical data from recent extreme events (such as the 2023 floods) to validate existing hydrodynamic models. These updated models should then be used to determine the safest and most effective locations and designs for new river gauges [25, 26].

## Cross-Cutting Connections

*How the research themes in this conference connect and interact.*

RESPONSE:
The research themes in this notebook—Infragravity Waves in Coastal Creeks, the 2023 DT01 Flood Damage Tool, and Emergency Resources—are deeply interconnected through a shared objective: **translating localized environmental data and economic risk assessments into actionable emergency management strategies in a changing climate**. 

Here are the cross-cutting issues, interdisciplinary connections, and specific papers that bridge these themes:

**1. Data Collection Infrastructure Enhancing Emergency Warning Systems (Bridging Infragravity Waves and Emergency Resources)**
The study of localized hydrodynamic phenomena relies heavily on monitoring technology, which directly feeds into emergency response capabilities. 
*   **Specific Paper Link:** The paper *"Infragravity Wave Considerations in Small Coastal Creeks"* by Newell, Rodger, and Smythe [1], [2] bridges these themes. The authors found that standard storm tide models failed to account for longer-period Infragravity (IG) waves, resulting in observed water levels up to 550mm higher than design levels [1], [2]. To address this, local councils installed low-cost water level gauges [3], [4]. 
*   **Connection:** The authors explicitly note that while these gauges were installed to measure IG waves and correct storm tide studies, they have the **crucial added benefit of providing real-time intelligence for flood warnings and the emergency management of Intermittently Closed and Open Lakes and Lagoons (ICOLLs)** [5], [6]. Empirical monitoring meant for scientific study directly becomes an emergency resource.

**2. Integrating Emergency Risk into Economic Modeling (Bridging the DT01 Flood Damage Tool and Emergency Resources)**
Historically, flood damage assessments focused primarily on property repair costs, but the intersection of economics and emergency management is becoming more formalized.
*   **Specific Paper Link:** The paper *"Benchmarking the 2023 DT01 Flood Damage Tool"* by Griffin et al. highlights how the newly adopted tool bridges economic damage assessments and emergency management [7], [8]. 
*   **Connection:** Unlike older models, **the 2023 DT01 Tool explicitly calculates "risk to life projected costs" based on the H1-H6 flood hazard categorisation** [9], [10]. Furthermore, it applies specific **"infrastructure uplift and emergency uplift"** factors to its economic damage calculations [11]. This interdisciplinary approach ensures that the financial costs of deploying emergency resources and safeguarding lives are mathematically factored into the Benefit-Cost Ratios (BCR) used to justify flood mitigation projects [12]. 

**3. Centralized Data Sharing Platforms (Bridging All Themes)**
A major cross-cutting issue is how isolated engineering studies (like coastal wave behavior or specific sub-catchment damage assessments) are communicated to the people on the ground responding to disasters.
*   **Specific Paper Link:** The paper *"NSW Flood Data Portal: from the beginning, linking critical data to disaster response"* by Daley and O'Fee illustrates this connection [13], [14].
*   **Connection:** The NSW Flood Data Portal acts as a centralized gateway where local councils and engineers upload hydraulic models, spatial data, and damage assessments [15], [14]. The NSW State Emergency Service (SES) then **uses this specific technical data—including the outputs of tools like the DT01—to inform operational sectors, plan evacuation routes, and issue community warnings** [15], [16], [14]. By standardizing how flood intelligence is stored, the portal turns abstract modeling and risk data into a tangible emergency resource.

**4. Compounding Hazards and Climate Adaptation**
A recurring interdisciplinary theme across the notebook is that historical data is no longer sufficient due to climate change and compounding natural disasters, requiring rapid adaptation in all three areas.
*   **Connection:** The DT01 Damage tool incorporates climate change scenarios and probability shifts to assess how flood damage will change in the future [17], [18]. Meanwhile, coastal creek models are being rewritten because traditional networks fail to capture extreme variations like IG waves [1], [4]. On the emergency front, researchers highlight how compounding hazards—such as extreme bushfires making catchments barren and more susceptible to severe flooding—are drastically altering flow dynamics, creating new challenges for emergency preparedness and resource allocation [19], [20], [21]. Tools like the Bureau of Meteorology's *Flood Scenarios Outlook* are actively bridging the gap between meteorological science and emergency practitioners to forecast these unpredictable, escalating events [22], [23], [24].

## Research Gaps

*Unanswered questions and future directions identified in the literature.*

RESPONSE:
The sources identify a wide range of research gaps, unanswered questions, and future directions across physical flood modeling, economic damage assessment, emergency communications, and compounding hazards. 

**Infragravity Waves and Coastal Dynamics**
*   **Gap/Need:** Historically, standard storm tide studies have failed to account for the presence of **Infragravity (IG) waves** (often called "surf beat") in small coastal creeks, leading to significantly underestimated design storm tide levels [1, 2]. Furthermore, the interaction between high ocean levels and heavy rainfall events remains poorly understood [3], and there is inconclusive evidence regarding how climate change will alter spatial patterns of rainfall [3].
*   **Future Directions:** Researchers and local councils are urged to explicitly incorporate specific analyses of IG waves into their coastal creek storm tide studies and to install low-cost gauge networks to collect local observation data [2, 4]. Additionally, future updates to sea level rise assessment tools need to incorporate AR6 low-confidence scenarios, historic high-tide inundation data, and allowances for increasing tide ranges [5].

**Flood Damage Tools and Economic Assessment (2023 DT01 Tool)**
*   **Gap/Need:** The adoption of the new 2023 DT01 Flood Damage Tool has revealed significant discrepancies with previous models, calculating **average residential damages 73% higher and non-residential damages 161% higher** [6, 7]. Furthermore, there is currently a lack of methods to properly quantify the economic damages caused by the inundation of open space and park infrastructure [8].
*   **Future Directions:** Because the 2023 DT01 Tool generates higher damage estimates, economic analyses for flood risk management options should be updated; options that were previously dismissed due to low Benefit-Cost Ratios (BCRs) may now prove economically viable [9]. For academics and economists, there is a specific call to "develop improved means for quantifying annualised damages for park inundation" [8].

**Emergency Management, Communications, and Community Resilience**
*   **Gap/Need:** A major unanswered question is how flood experts can effectively communicate risks, solutions, and forecast uncertainty to the wider community [10-12]. Regarding community resilience, researchers question whether the standard 'Know Your Flood Risk' (KYFR) advice is an easily achievable learning activity for households, and whether generic preparedness information is adequate for residents to respond to highly specific local risks [13, 14].
*   **Future Directions:** Future research must determine if different approaches to capability building are required for different at-risk populations, and how households' varying coping strategies alter their specific flood information needs [14]. Projects are currently underway to identify service gaps and develop strategies to improve the communication of flash flood risks and uncertainty terminology [11, 12].

**Compounding Hazards (Bushfires and Floods) and Climate Drivers**
*   **Gap/Need:** The impact of bushfires on the hydrological cycle—specifically how they increase catchment vulnerability to flooding and erosion—needs urgent assessment [15, 16]. 
*   **Future Directions:** The sources highlight a need for "modelling catchments after fire" [17]. Researchers recommend continued observation of hazard trends (distribution, frequency, magnitude, and return periods), conducting spatial and statistical analyses on climate data, and analyzing damage assessment data to understand the true severity of post-fire flood events [18].

**Infrastructure and Nature-Based Solutions**
*   **Gap/Need:** Despite extensive research, our **understanding of levee failure mechanisms**, their behavior during critical conditions, and the modeling of their failures remains uncertain [19]. Another open question is whether ecosystem-based riparian management (implemented since the 1980s) is successfully affecting flood flow hydrology at a regional scale [20]. Finally, it is highly difficult to predict the likelihood of **secondary risks** occurring in flood-isolated communities—such as medical emergencies, loss of services, and fire—or to determine what constitutes an "acceptable period of isolation" [21].
*   **Future Directions:** To address these gaps, researchers propose testing and refining National Guidelines for Nature-Based Solutions in diverse locations and establishing a national communications hub and community of practice [22]. In hydrology, preliminary investigations suggest that incorporating multiple-linear regression could improve flood frequency estimates, though this remains an open question needing further study [23]. Finally, targeted case studies are recommended to answer key gaps and contentious issues in local Floodplain Management Plans [24].
