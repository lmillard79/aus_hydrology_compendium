## Detailed Analysis

Presented at the Hydrology and Water Resources Symposium 2014—an event covering diverse topics ranging from **groundwater-surface water interactions and climate change to hydraulic modelling and water resource management** [1-3]—the paper *"A Review of the Broome Sandstone Aquifer to Enable Planning for Irrigation and Other Uses"* by Robert Paul, Richard George, and Peter Gardiner examines the viability of agricultural expansion in Western Australia's West Kimberley region [4]. 

**Overview of the Broome Sandstone Aquifer**
The Broome Sandstone aquifer is the **principal groundwater resource in the West Kimberley**, covering more than **30,000 km² along the coastal margin of the Canning Basin** [5, 6]. The aquifer is virtually flat-lying, **up to 200m thick, highly transmissive, and generally fresh**, with a transmissivity estimated between 2,000 and 3,000 m²/day [5, 7, 8]. 

Historically, the aquifer has supported pastoral stations, mining, tourism, and domestic water supplies, and it is considered a fundamental component of the **needs, beliefs, and value systems of the Yawuru, Karajarri, and Nyangumarta traditional owners** [5]. 

**The Catalyst for the Review**
There is growing interest from pastoralists and horticultural companies to expand irrigated agriculture in the La Grange area (south of Broome) [9, 10]. Recently, two horticultural properties were allocated **5.2 GL/yr of the total 50 GL/yr available to irrigate up to 600 hectares of land** [5, 11]. To ensure that any further expansion is viable and meets regulatory and community requirements, the Department of Agriculture and Food (DAFWA) initiated the **four-year La Grange project**, funded by Royalties for Regions, to review the aquifer's potential [5, 9].

**Resource Allocation and Management Challenges**
The 2010 La Grange groundwater allocation plan divided the area into two management subareas: La Grange north (allocation limit of 35 GL/yr) and La Grange south (allocation limit of 15 GL/yr) [12]. While the total allocation is set at 50 GL/yr, the review emphasizes that **any future allocation increases require detailed aquifer analysis** that recognizes cultural and environmental values [13]. The La Grange area features highly sensitive environments, including two Ramsar wetlands (Roebuck Bay and Eighty Mile Beach), a marine park, and several Aboriginal cultural sites tied to groundwater, such as *Jila* (permanent water sources) and *Lirri* (soaks) [14].

**Initial Findings and Ongoing Investigations**
The review identified critical information gaps regarding the aquifer's long-term water levels, recharge rates, and the location of the saltwater–freshwater interface [15, 16]. To address this, the field program initiated several actions with the following early findings:
*   **Airborne Electromagnetic (AEM) surveys** were flown over the region, showing a good spatial correlation between aquifer salinity and AEM conductance, and successfully mapping the saltwater interface in most areas [17, 18].
*   Data collected from 157 bores indicated that the **water quality is better than previously recorded**, and **groundwater levels have risen by approximately 1.5 metres since 1999** [19].
*   The data suggests that the aquifer has a more variable salinity distribution and finer-scale patterns of recharge and discharge than was previously understood [11].

To move forward, the authors note that all acquired field data will be used to **develop a groundwater model** [11]. This tool will be crucial for calculating the optimum spacing of new horticultural precincts to minimize interference and for evaluating the potential impacts of groundwater pumping on the region's vital environmental and cultural assets [11].
