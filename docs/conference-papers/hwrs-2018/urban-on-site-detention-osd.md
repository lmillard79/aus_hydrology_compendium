## Detailed Analysis

**Urban On-Site Detention (OSD)** is a critical component of modern urban stormwater management, functioning to ensure that new land developments and redevelopments do not cause adverse flood impacts on downstream properties [1, 2]. To achieve this, local government policies typically dictate a storage-based approach, requiring OSD systems to maintain post-development peak runoff at or below pre-development levels across a full range of design event frequencies and durations, up to a designated flood standard such as the 100-year event [1, 2].

**The complexities of OSD design** involve finely "tuning" the storage and release mechanisms to achieve peak flow mitigation across all required events. This often necessitates complex, multi-stage outlets utilizing orifice and weir controls, which can be challenging to balance because a system tuned to mitigate mid-duration events might struggle to adequately mitigate the very shortest or longest storms [3]. Furthermore, **matching pre- and post-development flows is not the only design requirement** [4]. Authorities typically mandate additional safety measures, such as maintaining a minimum 300mm freeboard from the peak 1% Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) water level to any adjacent building floor levels, providing capacity to handle partial outlet blockages, and undertaking detailed assessments of extreme overflow behavior [5]. 

Within the larger context of **HWRS 2018**, a significant research focus is placed on **how the updated Australian Rainfall & Runoff 2016 (ARR2016) guidelines impact OSD systems** that were originally designed using the previous ARR1987 standards [6, 7]. ARR2016 introduced a fundamental shift in design philosophy by replacing the older Average Variability Method (AVM) temporal patterns with a new ensemble approach, requiring an assessment of multiple temporal patterns per event to better represent natural rainfall variability [6, 8].

Researchers evaluated ten existing OSD systems across New South Wales, Victoria, and Queensland to understand the practical implications of this shift [7]. The findings offer several key insights for the stormwater industry:
*   **Continued Mitigation Performance:** OSD systems sized and designed under the older ARR1987 standards generally still perform their core function of mitigating peak flow increases to a similarly high standard under ARR2016 [9-11]. 
*   **Changes in Flow Characteristics:** When running models with the ARR2016 guidelines, the critical event durations were generally shorter, and the magnitude of peak outflows was actually lower than predicted under ARR1987 [10]. Peak flow reductions were found to be in the order of 10% to 15%, which is attributed to the fact that the ARR2016 ensemble patterns tend to be more back-loaded compared to the ARR1987 patterns [12, 13].
*   **Capacity and Freeboard Safety:** While the overall peak flows were lower, assessing the absolute worst-case scenario from the new ARR2016 ensembles showed that the majority of the evaluated OSD systems reached slightly higher peak volumes than they did under ARR1987 [14]. Despite this slight increase in peak volume, all case study systems remained within their modeled capacity without overtopping, indicating that the original ARR1987 storage sizings were generally adequate to handle the transition to ARR2016 [10, 15].

**Recommendations for Practitioners:** The ten-fold increase in design events resulting from the ARR2016 ensemble approach creates ambiguity in how results should be interpreted for OSD compliance [16]. The research recommends that practitioners adopt the **mean ensemble result** when assessing general peak flow mitigation, as this captures the central tendency of the ensemble [17, 18]. However, when evaluating **safety-critical aspects of OSD design**, such as maximum storage capacity and freeboard limits, designers should run a sensitivity check using the **worst-case ensemble result** to ensure the system is resilient against the full range of historical rainfall variability [17, 19, 20].
