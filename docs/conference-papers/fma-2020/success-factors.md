# Success Factors

**Conference:** FMA 2020 — Property-Based Floodplain Management  
**Theme 2 of FMA 2020**

---

## Overview

- Sound community engagement
- Giving homeowners options
- Strong local leadership
- Recent disaster impetus
- The 'Right Team' skills

---

## Detailed Analysis

Property-based floodplain management encompasses measures such as voluntary or compulsory house buy-backs, land-swaps, and house resilience or raising schemes designed to reduce risk-to-life and property damage [1, 2]. Because these programs interact directly with individuals' private dwellings and land, their successful implementation often presents greater social challenges than technical ones [3]. 

Research into historical property-based schemes identifies several key **success factors** that can guide floodplain managers:

*   **Effective Community Engagement:** Engaging with the community is vital to prevent misconceptions and educate residents about their flood risks and options [4]. For voluntary schemes, it must be emphasized that the final decision rests with the homeowner, and authorities must deliver on their commitments to build trust [4].
*   **Deploying the "Right Team":** The personnel tasked with approaching and discussing property purchases with the community must possess appropriate mediation, facilitation, and technical presentation skillsets [4].
*   **Capitalizing on Recent Disasters:** The recent experience of a flood event serves as a strong impetus, making property owners more willing to sell or relocate [4]. Having a plan that can be readily launched immediately after a disaster can assist with speedy recovery and encourage greater uptake of voluntary schemes [4].
*   **Strong Local Leadership and Resources:** Strong leadership at the local level can inspire and drive a community toward the difficult end-goal of relocation [4]. Additionally, utilizing local resources can save on budget while simultaneously reinvigorating the local economy following a disaster [4].
*   **Providing Options to Home-owners:** "Combination" schemes that give residents a choice—such as selecting between a land swap, house purchase, or house raising/rebuilding—give people a crucial sense of ownership and control after a disaster [4]. Providing financial counselling alongside these options can also assist decision-making and result in higher program uptake [4].
*   **Keeping Tight-Knit Communities Together:** Highly engaged communities often prefer land-swap schemes over individual property purchases so they can remain together [4]. In these cases, it is crucial that the new settlement location is geographically close to the original site [4].
*   **Assisted Relocations:** As an alternative to formal land-swaps, local governments can facilitate voluntary house purchases by using GIS technology and personalized consultation to help individual residents resettle within the local area [4, 5]. 
*   **Voluntary Operation within a Compulsory Framework:** Even when legislation allows for compulsory acquisition, executing purchases through mutual, voluntary agreement is often much more cost-effective than engaging in expensive and lengthy legal battles [4, 6]. 
*   **Flexible Social Purchase Criteria:** Sometimes it is warranted to purchase neighboring properties that do not strictly meet the technical flood criteria [4]. This is done to prevent dividing families or socially isolating neighbors when adjacent homes are bought out [4, 7]. 
*   **Preventing Panic Selling:** Implementing plans to manage and mitigate potential "panic selling" within the community helps support the overall stability and success of a house purchase program [4].
