## Detailed Analysis

**Land use planning, development controls, and building standards are widely considered the most effective tools available to reduce the impact of natural disasters on communities** [1]. The decisions made today regarding how flood-prone land is developed will continue to affect community resilience for generations, especially as climate change alters flood risk profiles [1, 2]. 

**Moving Beyond the 1% AEP Benchmark**
Historically, planning controls in Australia have focused almost exclusively on the 1% Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP) design flood event, but the sources emphasize that this is no longer sufficient [3]. The 1% AEP is now viewed as a "bare minimum standard," and an overreliance on it has left communities highly sensitive to climate-induced changes in flood risk [4, 5]. A contemporary, risk-based approach requires assessing the full range of possible flood events, including the Probable Maximum Flood (PMF) [2, 6]. 

For example, the New South Wales 2021 flood-prone land package explicitly provides a framework for councils to apply development controls on land between the 1% AEP and the PMF, acknowledging that risk management should not simply stop at the 1% AEP line [6-8]. **This is especially critical for vital infrastructure—such as electrical substations, hospitals, and tunnels—where a 1% AEP standard is deemed entirely inappropriate because these critical assets must remain functional during extreme events** [9].

**Key Planning Strategies and Controls**
The sources categorize land use planning strategies into three primary approaches, which are most effective when combined [10, 11]:
*   **Location Controls:** **Controlling where we build is arguably the most powerful tool for protecting life and property** [11, 12]. Location controls prevent new developments in high-risk floodplains and facilitate "managed retreat" (relocating existing properties outside the floodplain, often post-disaster) [12-14]. Limiting floodplain development could potentially decrease average community risk by 40% by 2050 [12].
*   **Floor Level Controls:** This involves setting minimum floor levels, often referred to as the Nominal Flood Protection Level (NFPL), to keep theoretical floodwaters below the habitable areas of a building [3, 15].
*   **Flood-Resilient Building:** This strategy promotes construction techniques and materials that can withstand inundation, such as raising electrical meter boards, using water-resistant wall materials (like acrylic render instead of gyprock), and ensuring adequate sub-floor drainage [3, 16-19].

**Adaptation Pathways to Manage Climate Uncertainty**
A major challenge in coastal and floodplain planning is the uncertainty surrounding the timing and severity of future sea-level rise and extreme weather, which frequently leads to "decision-making paralysis" [20]. To overcome this, the sources advocate for an **Adaptation Pathways approach, which builds flexibility into land use planning by establishing clear "signals," "triggers," and "thresholds"** [21-24].
*   **Signals** act as early warnings that an adaptation threshold is approaching, giving decision-makers sufficient lead time (e.g., 10 years) to secure funding and assess new planning controls or physical works [25, 26].
*   **Triggers** are the specific decision points at which a new adaptation pathway must be selected and implemented [22, 27, 28].
*   **Thresholds** represent the point at which the current flood risk becomes unacceptable, such as when an existing NFPL no longer provides a safety margin against the 1% AEP flood due to sea-level rise [15, 27]. 

By mapping out these pathways, planning schemes can undergo orderly, timely iterations to keep pace with measured environmental changes, avoiding both premature over-investment and acting too late to prevent serious impacts [21, 29]. Ultimately, communities facing severe long-term impacts will have to make a choice between complex, expensive engineering defenses and a planned retreat or relocation [30, 31].

**Frameworks, Precincts, and Emergency Management**
To support these strategies, governments are implementing broader mapping and assessment frameworks. This includes defining specific Flood Risk Precincts (FRPs) to apply suitable planning controls based on whether a risk is deemed "tolerable" or "intolerable" [32, 33]. Furthermore, sustainable urban design paradigms like "Sponge Cities" are being integrated into local planning to mandate permeable pavements, infiltration trenches, and the use of flood pathways for parks and open spaces [34, 35].

Finally, **Flood Emergency Management Plans (FEMPs) are commonly mandated in planning schemes to manage the residual risk of new developments** [36]. FEMPs aim to minimize the exposure of people and property and ensure new developments do not overburden emergency services [37]. However, the sources warn that if a site's specific flood behavior and vulnerability make a FEMP ineffective or unsafe, the underlying planning strategy must be to simply avoid developing the site in the proposed manner [38, 39].
