## Practitioner Insights

*Key recommendations for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals.*

RESPONSE:
Here are the key practical recommendations and actionable insights for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals, categorized by the main research themes:

### 1. Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs)
*   **Transition to Multi-Hazard, Regional Planning:** Move beyond isolated, single-hazard local plans. DAPs should evaluate the compounded risks of multiple hazards (e.g., riverine flooding, coastal erosion, landslides) across regional or catchment-wide scales [1-3]. This regional approach unlocks opportunities for larger-scale risk reduction projects that transcend local government boundaries and funding capacities [4].
*   **Implement "Adaptation Pathways":** Rather than static plans, use dynamic adaptation pathways that package complementary risk reduction options to be implemented over time [5, 6]. Base these pathways on specific "triggers" or thresholds, allowing communities and governments to sequence investments efficiently as climate change impacts unfold [7-9].
*   **Establish Cross-Jurisdictional Governance:** Form Regional Steering Committees comprising local, state, and federal stakeholders. A "One Government" approach is essential to align resources, avoid duplication, and prevent community engagement fatigue [1, 10, 11].

### 2. Modelling and Analysis
*   **Adopt "Whole-of-Catchment" Boundaries:** Hydrologic models should be defined by actual catchment and flow path boundaries rather than artificial administrative borders [12, 13]. This reduces edge-effect errors and significantly improves the reliability of emergency preparedness products like municipal flood plans [12, 14].
*   **Leverage Continuous Simulation (CS):** Traditional event-based modelling is often insufficient for understanding volume-driven floods or complex climate impacts. Implement CS to model long-term hydrological processes (rainfall, evaporation, soil moisture) through both wet and dry phases. This provides a more nuanced understanding of flood frequency, duration, and infrastructure resilience [15-17].
*   **Use Surrogate Models for Computational Efficiency:** High-resolution hydraulic models are computationally expensive. Use simplified "low-fidelity" models combined with Empirical Orthogonal Functions (EOF) analysis to select the most informative flood events for simulation. This approach can reduce computational costs by up to 97% while maintaining robust performance [18-20].
*   **Deploy Real-Time Impact Analytics via APIs:** Move from static PDFs to dynamic situational reporting during emergencies. Integrate real-time flood models (like DASH) with GIS exposure datasets to deliver automated analytics on the exact number of impacted properties, closed roads, and affected power substations hour-by-hour [21-24].

### 3. Infrastructure and Land Planning
*   **Apply Project Response Indicators (PRIs):** Shift away from generic hazard mapping towards PRIs. PRIs establish specific physical thresholds where individual elements (e.g., a 0.4m flood depth for people's safety on roads, or groundwater levels breaching floor levels) become vulnerable. This helps pinpoint exactly *where* and *when* management interventions must be triggered [25-27].
*   **Incorporate the "True Cost" of Natural Functions:** When conducting cost-benefit analyses (CBAs) for mitigation, explicitly quantify the value of Nature-Based Solutions (NbS). Assign monetary values to ecosystem benefits like riparian restoration, green open space, and stream restoration for nutrient banking to better justify funding for non-grey infrastructure [28-30].
*   **Re-evaluate Transport Infrastructure QDLs:** Recognize that transport infrastructure (highways, rail) cannot be held to the same Quantitative Design Limits (QDLs) as flood mitigation infrastructure. Transport projects cannot "trade" flood impacts for the common good, so practitioners must develop distinct compliance justification frameworks that focus on resilience and minimizing road closure times rather than strict zero-impact AEPs [17, 31, 32].

### 4. Governance and Programs
*   **Develop Post-Adoption Action Plans:** Do not wait until a flood study is formally adopted to figure out how to use it. Draft a "post-adoption" plan months in advance to immediately upload data to public portals, update planning schemes, and brief emergency responders, ensuring the new information translates into immediate community benefit [33-36].
*   **Modernize Community Communication with AI:** Use generative AI tools (such as customized conversational chatbots) to "laymanize" dense technical flood studies and guidelines. This democratizes knowledge, allowing residents to query localized flood maps and AEP results in plain English without needing technical expertise [37].
*   **Engage Vulnerable Communities on "Blue Sky" Days:** Build trust and share knowledge with Culturally and Linguistically Diverse (CALD) and Aboriginal communities long before a disaster hits. Employ trauma-informed engagement, integrate trusted community leaders into incident command centres, and continuously share simplified, translated hazard information [38-41].

## Cross-Cutting Connections

*How the research themes in this conference connect and interact.*

RESPONSE:
The research themes in this notebook—Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs), Modelling and Analysis, Infrastructure and Land Planning, and Governance and Programs—are deeply interconnected, reflecting a paradigm shift in floodplain management. The discipline is moving away from isolated, static engineering solutions towards dynamic, multi-hazard, and community-integrated approaches. 

Here are the cross-cutting issues and interdisciplinary connections bridging these themes:

**1. Translating Complex Models into Actionable Land-Use Planning (Modelling + Infrastructure & Land Planning)**
A major cross-cutting issue is the challenge of converting highly technical, computationally heavy flood models into practical tools for urban planners and developers. 
*   **Melbourne Water's Strategic Integration:** Melbourne Water's comprehensive catchment modelling program bridges these themes by using updated hazard data to directly inform the Victoria Planning Provisions, municipal housing strategies, and infrastructure design for new greenfield developments [1-3]. 
*   **City of Gold Coast's FFC Layer:** Researchers demonstrate how automated 1D/2D hydraulic modelling outputs were translated into a non-statutory Flood Flow Conveyance (FFC) layer [4]. This bespoke mapping product clearly delineates floodways and risks, guiding appropriate development without requiring small-scale developers to undertake complex hydraulic modelling themselves [5, 6]. 
*   **Perth's Sub-Regional Planning:** In highly constrained areas like the East of Kwinana, regional flood modelling was used to directly influence land capability assessments, answering critical questions about how much land can be safely urbanised and whether protective infrastructure like levees are viable [7-9].

**2. Managing Multi-Hazard Complexity and Climate Uncertainty (Modelling + DAPs + Land Planning)**
As climate change accelerates, traditional "stationary" models based solely on extreme rainfall are becoming obsolete. Planners now must account for "compound" or cascading hazards, linking modelling with state-level adaptation planning.
*   **Multi-Hazard Adaptation in NSW:** The Northern Rivers Disaster Adaptation Plan (DAP) perfectly bridges DAPs and Governance. Following catastrophic flooding and bushfires, this DAP integrates established flood and coastal risk management frameworks to assess compound risks (floods, bushfires, landslides, cyclones) across seven local government boundaries [10-13]. It establishes "adaptation pathways" to strategically guide long-term infrastructure investment [14, 15].
*   **Christchurch's Project Response Indicators (PRIs):** In New Zealand, engineers combined modelling of pluvial flooding, coastal inundation, rising groundwater, and earthquake-induced land settlement into a single framework [16-18]. Because traditional "damages" metrics were insufficient, they developed PRIs—quantitative thresholds for specific infrastructure (e.g., when a road or building becomes unsafe) that trigger proactive land-planning or infrastructure management responses [19-21].
*   **Non-Stationary Risk Frameworks:** Addressing the fact that climate change makes flood risk non-stationary, practitioners are proposing new decision-support frameworks that assess development proposals not just on static flood depths, but on changing hazards and broad socio-economic values over time [22-25].

**3. Collaborative Governance and Community-Led Resilience (Governance + DAPs + Infrastructure)**
The sources highlight that top-down, technocratic infrastructure projects often fail or face public backlash without inclusive governance and community engagement [26, 27]. 
*   **Flood to Flourish:** This project bridges Governance and Infrastructure by moving away from "predict and control" engineering. It focuses on co-designing flood resilience strategies with communities and Traditional Owners, ensuring that infrastructure, land use planning, and warning systems reflect actual community risk tolerance and cultural values [27-30].
*   **Partnership-Based Modelling:** Melbourne Water’s mapping program bridges Modelling and Governance by utilizing a "joint delivery framework" with 38 local government authorities. By co-funding and jointly managing the modelling through Project Control Groups, both local councils and the state share responsibility, ensuring the data is actually implemented into local planning schemes [31-34].
*   **Funding for Multiple Benefits:** Justifying infrastructure funding now requires crossing disciplines to assess social, environmental, and cultural outcomes. Using approaches like the "Six Capitals" framework, engineers must work alongside ecologists, heritage experts, and economists to secure multi-agency partnership funding for infrastructure projects [35-38].

**4. Real-Time Intelligence for Emergency and Infrastructure Management (Modelling + Infrastructure + Governance)**
Advancements in rapid modelling are transforming how government agencies govern disaster response and manage physical infrastructure during an event.
*   **Live Impact Analytics:** Systems like FloodMapp and DASH bridge Modelling and Governance by overlapping real-time hydrodynamic models with geographic exposure data (roads, substations, populations) [39, 40]. Instead of static maps, emergency managers receive live metrics through APIs detailing exactly how many properties need evacuation or how many kilometres of road are submerged, drastically reducing manual GIS processing [40-42].
*   **Regional Evacuation Modelling:** The Flood Evacuation Model (FEM 3.0) in the Hawkesbury-Nepean Valley bridges Modelling and Infrastructure by using agent-based traffic simulations to assess how floodwaters interact with complex road networks. This allows governance bodies to test hypothetical road upgrades and dynamically plan large-scale evacuation routes under changing flood conditions [43, 44].

## Research Gaps

*Unanswered questions and future directions identified in the literature.*

RESPONSE:
The provided papers highlight several evolving challenges, knowledge gaps, and directions for future research across the disaster adaptation and floodplain management sectors. 

### **Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs) and Response**
*   **Integrating DAPs with Existing Frameworks:** Because regional, multi-hazard Disaster Adaptation Plans (DAPs) are newly legislated in NSW, ongoing implementation must determine how to best leverage and integrate them with established, hazard-specific programs like the NSW Floodplain Management Program and Coastal Management Frameworks [1], [2], [3].
*   **Warning Response Dynamics:** There is a **scarcity of research into how psychological and sociological factors interrelate** to influence public response to early warnings [4]. Future research needs to shift away from "gauge-centric" approaches and instead focus on the dynamics of the *whole* warning response system—specifically addressing social capital, risk awareness, and community preparedness [5], [6].
*   **Driver Behaviour at Floodways:** Further research is required to understand **how drivers assess safety at flooded crossings**, and how factors like signage, lighting, education, and warning lead times can better support safe decision-making [7].

### **Modelling and Analysis**
*   **Continuous Simulation (CS) Challenges:** While CS offers deep insights into flood duration and frequency, it is currently limited by **technical challenges such as model stability during dry periods** and constraints within existing modelling software packages. Future work must develop actionable pathways to integrate CS into established industry workflows [8].
*   **Automated Floodplain Mapping:** Attempts to automate Functional Floodplain Mapping have revealed that **manual refinement is still highly necessary** in complex hydraulic environments, such as tidally influenced areas [9], [10]. Future guidance is urgently needed to define what constitutes an **acceptable cumulative flood level impact** and to determine appropriate roughness values for encroachment techniques [11]. 
*   **AI and Machine Learning Limitations:** Surrogate AI/ML models show promise for rapid flood prediction but currently lack **geographic generalisability across diverse regions**. Future research must overcome computational constraints, fuse multi-modal data (satellite, crowdsourced, hydrological), and link these models to real-time emergency response infrastructure [12], [13].
*   **Melbourne Water's Technical Gaps:** Strategic research is actively being targeted toward several modelling gaps, including **rain-on-grid modelling approaches, coastal flood modelling frameworks, empirical flow estimation in urban settings, and multi-level climate change risk assessments** [14], [15].
*   **Multi-Hazard and Groundwater Indicators:** When assessing compounding hazards, there is a **lack of literature to guide the selection of "Project Response Indicators" for shallow groundwater hazards** [16]. To build effective adaptation strategies, these metrics must be refined using the specific values and risk tolerances of affected communities [16], [17].
*   **Building Vulnerability Curves:** Geoscience Australia is actively working to fill gaps in analytical flood vulnerability models by researching specific building typologies, including **two-storey McMansions, blocks of flats, older single-storey commercial complexes, and CBD buildings with unknown basement levels** [18].

### **Infrastructure and Land Planning**
*   **Pacing with Climate Science:** A major unanswered question is **how static land-use planning systems can adapt to ever-changing climate science**. Because planning decisions lock in development at a single point in time, practitioners struggle to define what constitutes the "best available information" when climate models and flood projections are continually being updated [19], [20]. We are currently only "scratching the surface" on how to apply new climate-changed rainfall values to infrastructure design and planning [21].
*   **Ambiguous Infrastructure Design Criteria:** Vague language in flood design criteria for civil infrastructure (like highways and bridges) frequently causes project delays. Future industry discussions are recommended to outline more precise requirements for environmental assessments [22].
*   **Predicting Structural Failure:** Predicting exactly when and if a building will suffer structural failure due to complex flood forces (hydrostatic, hydrodynamic, debris loading) remains a significant challenge, requiring expanded research to improve damage assessments [23].
*   **Nature-Based Solutions (NbS) & Building Back Better (BBB):** The implementation of NbS for catchment-scale mitigation requires further investigation into overcoming **regulatory barriers, complex stakeholder coordination, and long-term maintenance requirements** [24]. Similarly, applying "Building Back Better" frameworks in agriculture faces barriers like grant limitations and financial constraints, prompting ongoing surveys and expert interviews [25]. 

### **Governance and Programs**
*   **Flood Risk to Life Tolerability:** A newly formed working group is investigating the **tolerability thresholds for flood risk to life**. Unanswered questions include how to assign monetary value to human life in damage assessments, how to accurately measure different communities' risk thresholds, and how these tolerances will shift in the future [26].
*   **Post-Adoption Model Utility:** There is a recognized need to investigate **what happens after a flood model is officially adopted**. Research and planning are required to ensure new flood data is actually translated into strategic council operations, risk management, and community communications [27], [28].
*   **Implementing Flood Studies into Planning:** There is a **massive backlog of completed flood studies that have not yet been implemented** into local planning schemes. Governance reviews are exploring how to fast-track this implementation, potentially by grouping studies into regional planning amendments [29], [30].
*   **The Insurance Affordability Gap:** Using the new National Insurance Dataset, researchers aim to use machine learning to estimate coverage gaps and longitudinally track how government mitigations and hazard exposure impact **the affordability and availability of flood insurance** over time [31], [32].
