# Mappable Constraints

**Conference:** FMA 2023 — Managing Flooding Beyond Maps  
**Theme 2 of FMA 2023**

---

## Overview

- Flood extents and frequencies
- Flood function (conveyance and storage)
- Flood hazard (depth x velocity)
- Flood range (scale relative to DFE)
- Emergency response classification (FERCCs)

---

## Detailed Analysis

**Mappable constraints** represent the specific, quantifiable elements of flood behaviour and land limitations that can be visually depicted to inform risk management. In the context of flood planning, these constraints typically include [1-3]:

*   **Flood extents of different floods:** Mapping the boundaries of floods with different frequencies helps establish a baseline for understanding how often specific areas are exposed to inundation [1, 4].
*   **Flood function:** This categorizes the specific role an area plays during a flood, such as flow conveyance (floodways and flowpaths) or flood storage [1, 3].
*   **Flood hazard:** This constraint maps the danger posed by the combination of peak flow depth and velocity. Hazard is highly dynamic, changing across the floodplain, throughout the duration of a single event, and between floods of different magnitudes [1, 3].
*   **Flood range:** This illustrates how flood extents, functions, depths, velocities, and hazards shift in relation to the scale of the flood compared to the Defined Flood Event (DFE) [2, 3].
*   **Flood Emergency Response Classification of Communities (FERCC):** Mapping these classifications helps disaster managers understand the variations in evacuation issues and the consequences flooding will have on different communities [2, 3].

**The Larger Context: Managing Flooding Beyond Maps**

While these mappable constraints are vital indicators of where impacts and risks may occur, they only tell part of the story [5]. A core theme of the *Managing Flooding Beyond Maps* framework is that **"maps alone do not provide a complete picture of the flood risk or the complexities that may influence its management"** [6]. Maps are essentially technical drawings that provide a particular type of information about a specific area, but managing the actual risk requires a much broader understanding [7].

To make truly informed decisions, floodplain managers must look beyond the drawn boundaries and consider a wide array of dynamic, physical, and social factors:

*   **Storm and Catchment Variables:** Flood risk is heavily influenced by unmapped variables such as antecedent weather conditions, the specific types of storms driving the flooding (e.g., East Coast Lows), and storm durations and patterns, which have a major influence on emergency management [8].
*   **Hydraulic and Infrastructure Complexities:** Practitioners must consider how flowpaths vary drastically between small, frequent floods and rare, extreme events [9]. They must also account for localized flood behaviour around hydraulic structures like bridges, the current condition of mitigation infrastructure, and the time it takes for mitigation benefits (including nature-based solutions) to be realized [9].
*   **Emergency Response and Human Impact:** A map cannot fully capture the human experience of a flood. Risk management must factor in the timing, rate of rise, and duration of the flood, as these dictate whether communities will be isolated or exposed to secondary risks like fires or medical emergencies [10]. It also requires understanding evacuation limitations—specifically, whether access routes will remain trafficable long enough for people to reach safety—and planning for disruptions to critical community services like power, communications, and water [8, 10]. 

Ultimately, while mapping constraints provides the technical foundation for understanding flood behaviour, managing the risk requires integrating these maps with real-world advice on community vulnerability, emergency planning, and the unpredictable nature of extreme weather events [6, 7].
