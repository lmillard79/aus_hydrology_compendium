# Two Component Extreme Value (TCEV) Distribution

## What this is

The Two Component Extreme Value (TCEV) distribution models a flood record as a mixture of two distinct populations — "ordinary" floods generated by common rainfall events, and "extraordinary" floods driven by rare, high-intensity events. Each population is modelled as a Gumbel distribution, giving a combined distribution with four parameters.

TCEV was developed in Italy by Rossi, Fiorentino, and Versace (1984) and adopted in Australian hydrology in the 1990s as an alternative to LP3 for catchments where a single distribution struggles to capture both the body and upper tail of the flood record simultaneously.

## Why it matters in Australian practice

Some Australian catchments — particularly in Queensland and northern Australia — exhibit bimodal flood behaviour. Tropical cyclone events and El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) cycles can produce distinct flood populations within the same record. Standard LP3 fitting may underestimate the upper tail if the extraordinary population is not explicitly modelled.

TCEV was explored in ARR 1987 as a potential standard method but was ultimately not adopted as the primary recommendation. It remains relevant in research contexts and for sites where LP3 gives implausible upper-tail estimates.

## Key concepts

**Two-population mixture**
The TCEV explicitly separates ordinary and extraordinary floods. The extraordinary component is characterised by a lower frequency of occurrence (parameter Λ₂ < 1) but higher magnitude. The relative weight of the two components determines how much the upper tail departs from the ordinary flood distribution.

**Parameter estimation**
TCEV has four parameters (Λ₁, θ₁, Λ₂, θ₂), making estimation more demanding than LP3 (three parameters). Regional estimation of TCEV parameters has been attempted in Australia but requires large homogeneous regions. At-site estimation is unreliable except with very long records.

**Regional application**
TCEV lends itself to regional frequency analysis — pooling records across a homogeneous region to estimate the extraordinary component. This was explored for Queensland in the 1990s.

**Comparison with LP3**
At moderate AEPs (10%–1%), LP3 and TCEV often give similar estimates. Differences emerge at rare AEPs (0.5% and rarer) where TCEV may give substantially higher estimates if an extraordinary population is present. The choice matters most for dam safety applications.

## Unresolved questions

- Whether TCEV parameter regionalisation is feasible for Australian climate regions with sufficient data
- How to objectively identify whether a flood record contains two distinct populations
- Whether TCEV or LP3 better represents the true upper tail of Australian flood distributions — definitive evidence is limited
- The relationship between TCEV extraordinary flood population and specific causal mechanisms (cyclones, east coast lows)
