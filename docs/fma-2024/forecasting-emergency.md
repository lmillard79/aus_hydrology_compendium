# Forecasting, Monitoring & Emergency Response

## What this is

The operational side of flood risk management — real-time prediction, catchment monitoring, and emergency response coordination. This field bridges hydrological modelling with emergency management, using distributed sensor networks, weather radar, and hydrological models to provide actionable warnings before and during flood events.

## Why it matters in Australian practice

Australia's flash-prone catchments and sparse gauge networks make real-time flood forecasting challenging. The 2022 eastern Australia floods demonstrated both the value and limitations of current systems — warnings were issued, but lead times were short and uncertainty was high. Improving forecasting, particularly for flash floods, and building resilient infrastructure that can respond to forecast information is critical for reducing flood fatalities and damage.

## Key concepts

**Real-Time Flash Flood Forecasting**
Flash floods develop rapidly — often within hours of heavy rainfall. Forecasting requires high-resolution rainfall estimation (radar), rapid catchment response models, and low-latency data transmission. The trade-off between forecast lead time and accuracy is acute for flash floods.

**Distributed Sensor Networks**
Internet-of-Things (IoT) devices, citizen science rainfall gauges, and remote sensing (satellite, aerial) are expanding data availability beyond traditional Bureau of Meteorology networks. Integration of heterogeneous data sources into operational forecasting systems is an active development area.

**Resilient Infrastructure**
Moving beyond static design standards to infrastructure that can respond to real-time information — floodable bridges, adaptive detention systems, or pre-emptive reservoir drawdown based on forecasts. This requires robust forecasting systems with quantified uncertainty.

**Compound Hazards**
Concurrent flooding with storm surge, extreme winds, or bushfire debris can produce worse impacts than flooding alone. Emergency response planning must account for compounding effects and cascading infrastructure failures.
