**Climate change is creating a dichotomy in urban and catchment flood responses** [1], [2]. Rising temperatures and intensified storms are generating more extreme rainfall patterns, leading to more destructive, rare floods [1], [3]. Conversely, longer dry spells are creating drier antecedent soil moisture conditions, which increases rainfall abstraction (losses) and actually decreases the severity of more frequent, "nuisance" floods [4], [1], [3], [5]. Studies show that both initial and continuing rainfall losses are projected to increase across almost all of Australia under future climate scenarios [6], [7], [8]. While these increased losses may help offset the impacts of increased rainfall for frequent flood events, they will be largely ineffective at buffering larger, extreme rainfall events [8], [9]. Furthermore, there is robust evidence indicating a **significant intensification of short-duration (sub-hourly) rainfall extremes**, with a positive trend of at least 20% per decade observed near Sydney—a trend that is accelerating faster than widely reported daily rainfall scales and severely increases flash-flooding risks [10], [11].

**The impacts of climate change on water supply are not just a future problem, but are already occurring** [12]. A case study in South-East Queensland demonstrated that discernible climate change impacts commenced in the early 1990s, where a 1.2°C increase in temperature resulted in a 30-40% reduction in annual streamflow, subsequently reducing the water supply system's yield by approximately 30% [12], [13], [14]. Traditional yield assessment methods assume climate stationarity; therefore, they will overestimate a system's yield and reliability when a drying trend exists [12], [15], [16]. To address this, practitioners are developing non-stationary stochastic models, such as the Temperature Covariate Method, which uses correlations between temperature and other climate variables to better account for evolving climate trends in yield assessments [12], [17]. Looking ahead, future water supply scenarios (such as those modeled for Tasmania out to 2070) reveal complex spatiotemporal trends, projecting wetter summers on the east coast, wetter winters in the west, and drier overall conditions in central regions, highlighting the ongoing uncertainty in water supply planning [18], [19].

**Future trends in hydrological modelling emphasize the abandonment of overly conservative assumptions in favor of dynamic, integrated, and AI-driven approaches.** For instance, when evaluating flood risks near the intersection of local catchments, regional waterways, and coastal boundaries, traditional "cumulative extreme" assumptions (e.g., assuming peak river flows, peak local runoff, and peak storm tides all occur simultaneously) are being replaced by joint probability methods. This avoids overestimating flood levels and unnecessarily burdening property owners and urban planning processes [20], [21], [22], [23]. Moreover, with climate change estimated to increase Probable Maximum Precipitation (PMP) by 13% to 33% by 2100, there is a recognized need to regularly update PMP estimation methods and their associated temporal patterns to ensure critical infrastructure is adequately protected [24]. Machine learning and Artificial Intelligence are also emerging as critical tools, demonstrating significant potential for long-term streamflow forecasting [25], predicting medium- to long-term rainfall anomalies using global climate indices [26], [27], and integrating with global forecasts to predict flood timing and uncertainty [28], [29].

**There is a necessary paradigm shift underway from flood resistance to flood resilience** [30]. Relying solely on structural mitigation measures (like levees and enlarged drainage conveyance) to "prevent" floods is becoming unaffordable, unsustainable, and can paradoxically increase long-term risk by encouraging new development in floodplains and creating a false sense of security in the community [31], [32], [33]. Future flood management strategies must incorporate non-structural measures aimed at reducing vulnerability, such as proactive land-use planning, building codes, targeted community education, preparedness programs, and insurance integration [34], [35], [36], [37].

Finally, **environmental water management is adapting to set "climate ready" ecological objectives** [38], [39]. Changing water availability, reduced groundwater recharge (predicted to fall by as much as 50% in some catchments by 2090), and sea-level rise are threatening ecosystems such as dryland and coastal wetlands [40], [41], [42]. Because historical climates and specific native species may no longer be viable under future conditions, ecohydrological management is shifting away from restoring "pre-regulation" regimes and instead prioritizing the health of ecological functional groups that can adapt to a warmer, highly variable future [43], [44].
