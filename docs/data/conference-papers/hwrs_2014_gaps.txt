**Broome Sandstone Aquifer Review**
*   **Identified Gaps:** There are significant information gaps regarding the operation and dynamics of the Broome Sandstone aquifer, particularly concerning long-term groundwater level trends, water quality, and hydraulic properties in areas where the aquifer interacts with agriculture and ecosystems [1]. Local data is highly limited and mostly confined to oil and gas records or early consultant reports, and there is a lack of broad-scale data on the region's Pindan soils [2], [3], [4].
*   **Future Directions:** A staged field program is currently addressing these gaps. Future work will focus on continuing Airborne Electromagnetics (AEM) inversion and field verification to inform recharge assessments [5], [6]. Researchers plan to acquire detailed bore datasets (including elevation and geochemistry) for aquifer characterization, install monitoring bores, conduct aquifer tests, and use data loggers to capture water level responses over the wet season [6]. Ultimately, a groundwater model will be developed to evaluate the potential impacts of pumping on environmental and cultural assets and to calculate the optimal spacing of horticultural precincts to minimize neighboring impacts [6].

**Monthly Streamflow Forecasting**
*   **Identified Gaps:** Current seasonal streamflow forecasting services generally provide three-month total volumes, but water agencies have expressed a strong need to have these broken down into monthly volumes and extended to longer stochastic lead times [7], [8], [9]. In developing ensemble streamflow forecasting systems, there is a recognized need to better quantify errors in the hydrological and error correction modelling to achieve reliable forecasts across all lead times [10]. Furthermore, preliminary stochastic scenario generation models have only been tested on three catchments, and the calibration of models (like WAPABA and seasonally variant error models) has historically been done without formal cross-validation [11], [12], [13].
*   **Future Directions:** Future research aims to extend the forecast horizon out to 12 months and to formally cross-validate the calibration of the hydrological and error models [12], [13]. Further testing is required across a wider variety of catchments, and researchers note the need to check seasonal cumulative volumes for skill and reliability [13]. Other goals include improving the reliability of streamflow forecasts at short lead times and examining alternative, potentially more skilful sources of forecast rainfall [14].

**Dhaka City Flood Management**
*   **Identified Gaps:** The conventional "19th-century solution" for stormwater management in Dhaka City—designed solely to rapidly collect and discharge runoff—is highly unsatisfactory because it disrupts the hydrological cycle, leads to severe flooding, pollutes waterways, and wastes a valuable water resource [15], [16]. Rapid, unplanned urbanization has severely reduced natural wetlands (from 77% to 19%), drastically limiting natural infiltration and compounding waterlogging issues [17].
*   **Future Directions:** Researchers suggest abandoning this conventional approach in favor of an **"Infrastructure Compliant Stormwater Management" (ICSM) strategy** incorporating Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD) technologies, such as leaky wells, soakaways, and infiltration trenches [16], [18], [19]. Future implementations should focus on applying these WSUD measures across the Dhaka floodplain to provide 100-year Average Recurrence Interval (ARI) flood protection while simultaneously increasing aquifer recharge [19], [20].

**Rainfall Disaggregation Vietnam**
*   **Identified Gaps:** In Asian monsoon catchments like the Ba River in Vietnam, there is a lack of continuous sub-daily rainfall records required for reliable flood flow simulation; typically, only daily-read gauge data is available [21]. When using the "Method of Fragments" (MoF) to disaggregate this daily data, a major limitation is the repetition of patterns (creating a cyclic output) if the sub-daily dataset is small compared to the number of days requiring disaggregation [22].
*   **Future Directions:** To resolve this limitation, future models should alleviate repetition by extending the fragment dataset using data from nearby stations [22]. This approach will create more diverse options for the disaggregation of large rainfall events, which is critical for accurate hydrologic flood modelling [22].

**Turbulence in Non-Uniform Flow**
*   **Identified Gaps:** Turbulence intensities in steady non-uniform flows have received little investigation compared to uniform flows [23]. While previous studies noted that non-uniform turbulence intensity deviates from standard linear distributions due to vertical velocity, vertical velocity is generally too small to measure in practice. This makes existing theories difficult for ordinary engineers to apply to practical problems [24], [25]. Prior to this research, there was no universal model to express these distributions in complex flow conditions [23].
*   **Future Directions / Addressed Questions:** The authors identified the need for—and subsequently developed—a universal predictive model that relies on mean stream-wise velocity and flow acceleration, entirely bypassing the need for vertical velocity [25], [26], [27]. Moving forward, this framework demonstrating that flow acceleration causes the deviation of turbulence intensities (concave when acceleration is negative, convex when positive) can be broadly applied to complex, steady non-uniform flows to estimate the full profile of horizontal and vertical turbulence intensities from the channel bed to the free surface [28], [29], [30].

**Other Research Gaps Mentioned in the Symposium**
*   **Rating Curve Errors:** More research is required to understand the implications of rating curve extrapolation errors on flood quantile estimates, as most hydrologic models erroneously treat at-site flood data as perfectly "accurate" measurements [31].
*   **Climate Change and IFD Estimates:** Current climate change research lacks a coordinated, national focus and is not sufficiently developed to provide the advice necessary to develop Intensity-Frequency-Duration (IFD) estimates for possible future climates [32].
*   **Probable Maximum Precipitation (PMP) Estimation:** Aspirational future research areas for PMP estimation include validating PMP estimates through independent data, quantifying the uncertainty associated with operational estimates, testing the validity of current techniques under a changing climate, and determining the feasibility of using radar data, non-systematic data, or physical models in operational derivations [33], [34].
