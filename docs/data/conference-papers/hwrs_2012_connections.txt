**Software and systems act as the primary integrative framework** that binds together hydraulic modelling, flood estimation, water management, and environmental studies. Modern modelling platforms are no longer siloed into specific hydrological sectors; instead, they are designed to simulate the full land phase of the hydrological cycle [1]. 
*   **eWater Source** is a prime example of this interdisciplinary connection, functioning as a catchment model, hydrodynamic model, and water quality model. It integrates daily rainfall-runoff generation (Flood Estimation), the physical flow of water (Hydraulic Models), constituent loading like nitrogen and phosphorus (Environmental Studies), and complex entitlement-based water sharing rules (Water Supply and Demand Management) [1-4].
*   **The Computer Aided River Management (CARM) system** for the Murrumbidgee River bridges multiple themes by integrating Bureau of Meteorology rainfall forecasts, real-time extraction metering, and integrated surface-groundwater hydrodynamic models (MIKE 11 and MIKE SHE). This system automatically updates model states to emulate real river behaviour, optimizing dam releases to meet irrigation demands while minimizing operational surplus and targeting environmental wetland watering [5-7].

**Bridging Design Flood Estimation and Hydraulic Modelling**
While statistical flood frequency analysis and joint probability approaches (Design Flood Estimation) determine the magnitude and likelihood of a flood, hydraulic models are required to translate those flows into physical water levels, velocities, and inundation extents. 
*   **The Nerang River Catchment study** provides a specific example of bridging these two themes. Researchers integrated the complex hydrodynamic attributes of a tidal floodplain directly into a conceptual hydrologic model. Because running a full 2D hydraulic model thousands of times for a Monte Carlo joint-probability analysis is computationally prohibitive, they ran the hydraulic model to create a multi-dimensional "look-up table." This allowed the hydrologic model (RORB) to rapidly estimate peak floodplain water levels based on stochastically sampled rainfall, reservoir drawdowns, and tide levels [8-10].
*   **Tackling "Non-Hydrological Behaviours" (NHB):** In real-time flood forecasting, conceptual hydrological models (like URBS) often struggle with complex hydraulic interactions, such as backwater effects, tidal influences, or floodwaters breaking out into floodplains and returning later. Research bridges this gap by mimicking these complex hydraulic and hydrodynamic processes within simpler, event-based hydrological models using physically-based conceptual loss and routing commands, ensuring timely and accurate flood warnings without the computational burden of full 1D/2D hydraulic models [11-13].

**Balancing Consumptive Water Supply with Environmental Demands**
Water supply management and environmental studies are increasingly intertwined as basin authorities shift from viewing environmental flows as mere "minimum passing flows" to actively managing environmental water portfolios alongside consumptive users.
*   **Environmental Portfolio Optimisation:** New optimization tools are being developed to explicitly link **River System Models** with **Ecological Response Models (ERMs)**. By treating Environmental Water Requirements (EWRs) as a quantifiable demand, optimization models simulate decades of climate sequences to determine how to best trade, carry over, and release environmental water across multiple assets to maximize ecological health without shortchanging consumptive users [14-17].
*   **The MacKenzie River study** exemplifies this interdisciplinary connection. Researchers developed a framework to configure consumptive water transfers (Water Supply) in a way that generates "pulses" or "freshes" to maximize the ecological response of aquatic ecosystems, specifically looking at algal periphyton communities and channel integrity (Environmental Studies). This represents a shift from a contest over water volume to a cooperative approach with multiple socioeconomic and environmental benefits [18, 19].

**Climate Change, Variability, and Model Uncertainty**
The assumption of climatic stationarity is a cross-cutting issue that challenges historical design flood estimates, water supply yields, and environmental baselines. 
*   **Testing Model Validity Under Climate Change:** Researchers are heavily focused on how changing climate characteristics (like the "Millennium Drought") invalidate the assumption that models calibrated to historical runoff data will accurately predict future water availability. Studies crash-test hydrological models under contrasting wet and dry climate conditions to assess parameter stationarity and structural uncertainty, bridging the gap between historical flood estimation and future supply management [20-22].
*   **Impact on Water Quality and Hydrodynamics:** Climate change connects environmental quality with hydrodynamic parameters. For instance, a 3D hydrodynamic and water quality model applied to Lake Mead (USA) evaluates how climate-induced flow reductions and increased temperatures will alter hydrodynamics, residence times, salinity, and algal productivity, directly impacting the management of water supply for 20 million people [23, 24].
