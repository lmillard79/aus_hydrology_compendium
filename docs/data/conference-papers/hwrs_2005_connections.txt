RESPONSE:
The research themes presented at the 29th Hydrology and Water Resources Symposium (2005) are deeply interconnected, reflecting a paradigm shift in Australian water resources management towards integrated, interdisciplinary approaches. Rather than existing in silos, these themes continuously overlap to address the overarching goal of sustainable catchment management. 

**Modelling and Simulation** acts as the central pillar connecting all other themes. Whether researchers are predicting the impact of climate variability on storage, evaluating the design of urban stormwater wetlands, or assessing the physical conditions of river habitats, they rely on advanced modelling frameworks to quantify uncertainties and support decision-making.

Here is a comprehensive breakdown of the cross-cutting issues and specific examples of papers that bridge these distinct research themes:

### Cross-Cutting Issues and Interdisciplinary Connections

**1. Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD) and Triple-Bottom-Line Assessment**
The push for sustainable development bridges **Urban Hydrology**, **Stream Physical Conditions**, and **Modelling and Simulation**. Urban infrastructure is no longer just about flood mitigation; it is about treating stormwater at the source to protect the physical and ecological conditions of downstream receiving waters. Furthermore, evaluating these systems now incorporates economic and social factors—a "triple-bottom-line" (TBL) approach [1, 2]. 

**2. Ecological Responses to Hydrological Change**
Understanding how biological organisms (like fish and macroinvertebrates) respond to changes in streamflow and hydraulic habitats bridges **Hydrologic Sensitivity**, **Stream Physical Conditions**, and **Modelling**. Researchers are combining biological field data with hydraulic models to generate "ecological response rules" to ensure that environmental flows maintain in-stream heterogeneity [3-5].

**3. Uncertainty and Risk in a Variable Climate**
The extreme natural variability of Australia's climate connects **Climate and Storage** directly with **Modelling and Simulation**. Because historical records only provide a single realization of the climate, stochastic climate generation models are heavily utilized to synthesize rainfall data. These models account for multi-decadal variability (such as ENSO and the Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation) to better estimate flood risks, design dams, and manage long-term water storage [6-9].

### Specific Papers Bridging Multiple Themes

**Bridging Modelling, Urban Hydrology, and Stream Physical Conditions**
*   **The Catchment Modelling Toolkit (Perry et al. & Argent et al.):** The development of the Catchment Modelling Toolkit serves as a universal bridge across themes. It integrates diverse models to assess the whole-of-catchment impacts of land-use and water decisions [10, 11]. For example, the Toolkit houses the *Model for Urban Stormwater Improvement Conceptualisation (MUSIC)* for **Urban Hydrology**, the *River Analysis Package (RAP)* for evaluating **Stream Physical Conditions**, and the *Stochastic Climate Library (SCL)* for **Climate and Storage** applications [12, 13].
*   **Triple-Bottom-Line Assessment of Urban Stormwater Measures (Taylor et al.):** This paper bridges **Urban Hydrology** and **Modelling** by introducing a life-cycle costing module into the MUSIC model. It allows urban water managers to conceptually design structural stormwater measures (like constructed wetlands and bioretention systems) that improve **Stream Physical Conditions**, while simultaneously evaluating the ecological, social, and financial impacts of the infrastructure [2, 14].
*   **Modelling Performance of Urban Stormwater Wetlands (Li et al. & Taylor et al.):** These studies bridge **Urban Hydrology**, **Modelling**, and **Stream Physical Conditions** by tracking how pollutants (like total suspended solids and dissolved nitrogen) are transported and treated through constructed wetlands [15, 16]. Accurate modelling of these processes is critical to prevent the eutrophication of natural receiving waters [17, 18].

**Bridging Hydrologic Sensitivity, Climate and Storage, and Modelling**
*   **Modelling Hydrologic Sensitivity to Climate Conditions (Chiew et al.):** This paper connects **Hydrologic Sensitivity**, **Climate**, and **Modelling** by using two rainfall-runoff models (AWBM and SIMHYD) to assess how sensitive mean annual runoff is to projected climate changes (specifically rainfall and potential evapotranspiration). It provides a critical tool for predicting the impacts of global warming on future water storage and resources [19, 20].
*   **Estuarine Flood Levels and Joint Probability (Tan et al.):** This study combines **Modelling**, **Climate and Storage**, and **Hydrologic Sensitivity** by developing a generic Monte-Carlo based joint probability method. It integrates daily stochastic rainfall, conceptual rainfall-runoff models, and coastal ocean level models to estimate the annual exceedence probability of extreme floods in complex estuarine systems subject to multiple, interacting climatic forces [21, 22].

**Bridging Stream Physical Conditions, Hydrologic Sensitivity, and Modelling**
*   **Using RAP to Predict Biotic Response (Marsh et al.):** This paper uses the River Analysis Package (RAP) to link **Modelling** with **Stream Physical Conditions**. It establishes an "Ecological Response Models" module that translates predicted changes in hydraulic habitat (caused by human flow alterations) into specific ecological consequences for aquatic biota, such as the spawning habitats of rainbowfish [4, 5, 23].
*   **Hydrological Data Requirements for Large-Scale Ecological Studies (Costelloe et al.):** Bridging **Hydrologic Sensitivity** and **Stream Physical Conditions**, this study applies a conceptual water balance model to generate daily discharge data for arid zone rivers in the Lake Eyre Basin. By correlating modeled hydrological variables (like flow duration, timing, and amplitude) with biological measures (like fish, algae, and waterbirds), the researchers identify how sensitive arid ecosystems are to both natural flow regimes and large regional floods [24-26]. 
*   **Groundwater-River Interactions (Ivkovic et al.):** This study bridges **Stream Physical Conditions** and **Hydrologic Sensitivity** by classifying river reaches based on their hydraulic connection to aquifers. It demonstrates how groundwater extraction (a sensitivity driver) can deplete surface flows, alter the baseflow signature of a stream, and subsequently impact environmental flow releases and riverine ecosystem health [27-29].
