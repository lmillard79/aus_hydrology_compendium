QUERY:
What are the key practical recommendations for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals? What actionable insights can practitioners apply immediately?

============================================================

RESPONSE:
**Hydrologic Sensitivity**
*   **Use simple linear relationships for rapid climate impact assessments:** Practitioners can use a simple linear relationship to estimate the percentage change in mean annual runoff based on predicted percentage changes in mean annual rainfall and potential evapotranspiration (PET) [1, 2]. 
*   **Prioritize rainfall accuracy over PET:** Modelled runoff is between three to five times more sensitive to rainfall than to PET [1, 3]. While hydrological modelling should be conducted when calibration data is available, the choice of the specific rainfall-runoff model is less critical when assessing the impact of rainfall changes on mean annual runoff [4]. 
*   **Account for heightened sensitivity in dry catchments:** Planners should note that runoff sensitivity to rainfall is significantly higher in regions with low rainfall and low existing runoff, as well as in catchments with small effective storage capacities [1, 5, 6].

**Stream Physical Conditions**
*   **Align in-stream habitat structures with biological life stages:** When using large woody debris to encourage pool scour and restore habitat diversity, traditional engineering approaches that solely predict maximum scour depth are of limited use [7, 8]. Instead, practitioners must mesh long-term hydrology with hydraulics and biological requirements (e.g., critical spawning periods) to properly evaluate the ecological effectiveness and permanence of scour pools [9-11].
*   **Adopt visual flow-typing for rapid broad-scale hydraulic surveys:** To assess physical stream conditions over large areas efficiently, practitioners should use cross-sectional surface flow-typing (visually identifying dominant biotopes like deadwater, glide, or rippled flow) [12-14]. This rapid visual assessment serves as a highly reliable surrogate for Froude number and the depth-velocity environment, taking a fraction of the time of a full channel profile survey [14, 15]. Assessing approximately 15 cross-sections per kilometer is recommended [14, 16].
*   **Exercise caution with reach-scale parameters in upland rivers:** While reach-scale dimensionless hydraulic parameters (such as the Froude number) adequately reflect hydraulic heterogeneity in lowland regions, they should be applied with caution in upland streams due to inherently greater habitat and substrate variability [17-19].

**Modelling and Simulation**
*   **Avoid relying on single static models:** Catchment mechanisms change dynamically depending on antecedent conditions, meaning a single rigid model structure can introduce significant bias [20, 21]. Practitioners should employ a Hierarchical Mixtures of Experts (HME) framework, which probabilistically combines outputs from multiple model configurations based on current catchment states (e.g., wet vs. dry periods), yielding significantly better performance [21-23].
*   **Keep models conceptually appropriate and accessible:** Models should be no more elaborate than necessary to answer specific management questions at the required spatial scale [24, 25]. Ensuring that models remain relatively simple enhances their accessibility to stakeholders and facilitates participatory learning and quality assurance [24, 26].
*   **Use physically based predictors for stormwater wetlands:** When predicting sediment trapping efficiency in stormwater wetlands, practitioners should conceptualize the wetlands as a series of ponds and use the dimensionless particle fall number ($N_f$) rather than traditional first-order kinetic decay models [27-29]. This approach is physically based and relies less on arbitrary flow and sediment constants [30].

**Climate and Storage**
*   **Manage delayed impacts of groundwater extraction:** Because groundwater and surface water are interconnected, pumping groundwater from connected systems will ultimately deplete streamflows [31-33]. Because there can be a time lag of decades before streamflow impacts are fully realized, practitioners must implement a zonal management approach based on the *time lag of unacceptable impact* rather than physical distance alone [34-36]. Volumetric restrictions, seasonal rosters, or winter-fill licences should be instituted well in advance of impacts appearing [37-39].
*   **Account for multidecadal climate variability in flood risk:** Flood data stratifies heavily across multidecadal climate phases, such as the Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation (IPO) [40, 41]. Relying on short flood records that span only one IPO phase will yield biased estimates of long-term flood risk [42, 43]. Instead, managers should utilize regional flood frequency distributions derived from datasets that contain sufficient samples from both positive and negative climate epochs [43].

**Urban Hydrology**
*   **Do not apply a single Areal Reduction Factor (ARF) to whole catchments:** When estimating urban design floods using ARFs, adopting a single reduction factor based on the overall catchment area can underestimate peak flows by up to 5% and locally underestimate 100-year ARI flood levels [44-47]. Instead, applying a uniform ARF distribution across the catchment is recommended to more safely estimate the envelope of peak flows [46, 48].
*   **Optimize stormwater wetlands for dissolved nitrogen:** Nitrogen in urban runoff is predominantly in dissolved form (approximately 77%) [49]. To maximize treatment performance, stormwater wetlands must be specifically designed to remove dissolved nitrogen and be robust enough to handle highly variable, stochastic inflow concentrations that occur during both storm events and base flows [49]. 
*   **Implement pervious pavements for lightly loaded infrastructure:** Pervious pavements are a highly viable WSUD alternative that significantly reduce peak flowrates and runoff volumes (generating as little as 35% of the runoff of impermeable asphalt) [50, 51]. They also act as an excellent source-control treatment, intercepting heavy metals, up to 87% of solids, and up to 90% of hydrocarbons [52, 53].
*   **Standardize Triple-Bottom-Line (TBL) assessments:** Urban stormwater managers should employ TBL assessments using Multi-Criteria Analysis (MCA) as a decision-support tool to balance financial, social, and ecological factors [54-56]. The life-cycle costing module in the MUSIC model can be used immediately by practitioners to predict the costs of structural stormwater measures accurately during the conceptual design stage [55, 57-59].
