RESPONSE:
Here are the key practical recommendations and actionable insights for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals, drawn directly from the HWRS 2022 research themes:

### 1. Flood Estimation & Hydraulic Modelling
*   **Move Beyond 1D Cross-Sections to 2D SGS:** The industry is moving toward replacing 1D open channels with 2D Sub-Grid Sampling (SGS) cells and quadtree mesh refinement, which better represent real-world topography and cell storage [1, 2]. However, when doing so, practitioners must adjust structure losses based on approach and departure velocity fields to ensure accurate mass and momentum transfer [3]. 
*   **Always Check 2D Cell Size Convergence:** Using overly coarse 2D cell sizes can create artificial blockages and inaccurately raise simulated flood levels [4]. Modellers must perform cell size sensitivity tests to ensure results have converged, preventing unrealistic water mounding and velocities [5].
*   **Re-evaluate the "Zero Afflux" Requirement:** Authorities increasingly demand "zero afflux" (0 mm change) for development approvals, but achieving this is practically impossible due to inherent tolerances in Lidar topography (±0.1m to 0.3m), rainfall data, and model parameters [6-9]. Practitioners should advocate for policies based on "legal reasonableness" and cumulative tolerances rather than strict zero-millimeter thresholds [10, 11].
*   **Use Hydrologic Pre-Processing to Save Time:** Running thousands of rainfall ensemble combinations through a 2D hydrodynamic model is computationally expensive. Hydrologists should use hydrologic models (like WBNM) as pre-processors to identify a small selection of "within-tolerance" critical events, which can reduce hydraulic simulation runs from over 1,000 down to fewer than 5 [12-14].
*   **Direct Rainfall (Rain-on-Grid) is Viable but Requires Care:** Using 2D models for direct rainfall-runoff simulation is highly effective but requires rigorous calibration of Manning’s *n* values, cell sizes, infiltration, and subsurface flows (e.g., using Darcy's law equations in 2D) to accurately capture groundwater and baseflow mechanisms [15-18].

### 2. Climate Change & Uncertainty
*   **Abandon the "Stationarity" Assumption:** Infrastructure has traditionally been designed assuming the future will replicate the past, but anthropogenic climate change means stationarity is "dead" [19, 20]. Practitioners must evaluate infrastructure against a range of plausible non-stationary futures to avoid the risk of "climate change litigation" [20, 21].
*   **Adopt "Scenario-Neutral" Stress Testing:** Instead of relying on single top-down climate projections (which have high uncertainty), apply "scenario-neutral" stress tests (such as the foreSIGHT framework) [22, 23]. This involves perturbing historical hydroclimatic data across a wide range of attributes (e.g., rainfall, evaporation) to identify the specific "failure modes" and vulnerabilities of a water system [24-26].
*   **Apply Temperature-Based Rainfall Adjustments:** For event-based flood risk assessments, apply a scaling factor to current design rainfall intensities based on global warming sensitivity. A recommended formula to calculate future intensity ($IF$) is $IF = IP \times 1.05^{\Delta K}$, where $IP$ is present intensity and $\Delta K$ is the absolute annual mean temperature change [27, 28]. 

### 3. Urban Water Security
*   **Adopt an "Operational Yield" Approach:** Traditional static yield estimates are becoming obsolete due to changing climates. Water planners should transition to an "operational yield" approach, which models system behavior starting from *current* storage conditions, incorporating progressive climate change and future demand growth to pinpoint the exact future time points when a system will fail [29-32].
*   **Use Stochastic Sequences to Define Drought Triggers:** Planners should use multi-site, multi-season synthetic climate data generators (e.g., MSSSCAR) to simulate thousands of years of synthetic droughts [33, 34]. This enables the creation of "yield diagrams" that calculate the exact probability of hitting critical storage levels, providing clear lead times for when emergency drought infrastructure (like desalination) must be triggered [35, 36]. 
*   **Develop Community-Agreed Levels of Service (LoS):** Water security objectives should be developed alongside the community to ensure they are affordable and practical [37, 38]. Clear LoS metrics—such as the acceptable frequency, severity, and duration of water restrictions—must be established well before droughts occur [39, 40].

### 4. Data Science & Real-Time Forecasting
*   **Deploy Machine Learning (ML) Surrogate Models for Real-Time Warnings:** Physics-based 2D hydrodynamic models are highly accurate but too slow for real-time flood warning [41, 42]. Practitioners can train ML surrogate models using historical runs from hydraulic models (like TUFLOW). Once trained, the ML model can ingest real-time rainfall "nowcasts" and output highly accurate spatial flood maps in seconds rather than hours (e.g., an average error of ~2cm) [43-45].
*   **Use Hybrid Methods for Operational Rainfall Data:** Real-time flow forecasting often suffers from sparse or scarce rain gauge data [46, 47]. Practitioners should utilize hybrid methods that disaggregate gridded daily rain (like AWAP) using Thiessen-weighted gauge patterns. This makes operational models highly resistant to real-time data interference and performs significantly better than relying on raw gauge data alone [48-50].
*   **Leverage AI for Complex Hydrological Variables:** Consider using Decision Tree (DT) algorithms to predict complex, non-linear variables like monthly pan evaporation. DT algorithms can process large datasets efficiently and have proven capable of providing highly accurate estimations using only simple input parameters like solar radiation and mean air temperature [51-53].
