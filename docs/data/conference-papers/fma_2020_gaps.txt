RESPONSE:
The provided sources identify several research gaps, unanswered questions, and future directions related to property-based floodplain management, particularly concerning the economic, social, and communicative aspects of these schemes:

**Quantifying Intangible Costs and Benefits**
A significant gap identified is the **failure to adequately capture intangible impacts in cost-benefit analyses for retreat options**, such as voluntary house purchases [1]. If these intangible benefits are not measured, the overall benefits of retreating are underestimated, leading to lower adoption of these schemes [1]. To address this, further research and development are needed regarding "rapid" assessment techniques for non-direct property damages, such as social impacts, environmental impacts, and risk to life, which will assist in creating comprehensive future economic guidance [2-4].

**Longevity and Tracking of House Resilience**
For house resilience schemes involving modifications like house raising or wet-proofing, there is a recognized challenge regarding the **longevity of the scheme outcomes** [5]. Future investigation and systematic measures are needed to **prevent the loss of knowledge when properties transfer ownership** [5]. The sources suggest exploring standardized solutions, such as adding notes on property flood reports, to ensure new owners remain aware of the resiliency measures that were previously undertaken [5].

**Social Challenges and Community Preservation**
The literature highlights that the most crucial factor in relocation schemes is maintaining a sense of community, and failing to acknowledge these bonds significantly diminishes the take-up of voluntary schemes [1]. Future strategies need to explore **better ways to keep tight-knit communities together** [6]. One successful direction for further application and study is the use of GIS to help families identify suitable, local houses for resettlement, which helps preserve the community while also preventing local governments from losing tax revenue [7]. Additionally, more investigation is needed into finding **beneficial, income-generating uses for vacated land**, as communities often struggle with the restricted use of the open space left behind after flood-prone properties are purchased and demolished [1, 8].

**Communication of Mitigation Terminology**
Effective public consultation is critical for gaining acceptance of property-based programs, yet the **translation of technical terms specifically for the ‘mitigation’ phase of the disaster management cycle is currently "poorly researched"** [6, 9]. Future research is needed to establish agreed national or state-wide non-technical definitions for seemingly simple concepts (like "floodwaters"), as studies show even professional floodplain managers currently hold differing definitions [9]. 

**Impacts of Climate Change**
Finally, the sources emphasize that as climate change drives more global discussions around 'retreat' and 'adaption', **the implementation and relevance of property-based management schemes will likely increase in the future** [10]. Consequently, the lessons learned from historic relocations and buy-backs are intended to serve as a baseline and a starting point for further continuous research during the preparation of new schemes [6, 10].
