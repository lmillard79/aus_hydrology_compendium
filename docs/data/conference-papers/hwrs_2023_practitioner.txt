Here are the key practical recommendations and actionable insights for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals, drawn from the HWRS 2023 proceedings. 

### 1. Flood Forecasting and Warning Systems
*   **Integrate Radar with Traditional Gauges:** Do not rely solely on point-location rain gauges, as they can miss highly localized, short-duration extreme events. **Incorporate radar-based ensemble rainfall forecasts** (such as the Bureau of Meteorology’s Rainfields) into your hydrological models [1-3]. This allows for very frequent updates (every few minutes instead of hours) and significantly improves the spatial and temporal capture of evolving flash floods [3-6].
*   **Deploy Ultra-Fast Hydrodynamic Modelling for Real-Time Alerts:** Traditional 2D hydraulic models are often too slow for real-time flash flood warnings. Practitioners should adopt **cloud-based, ultra-fast hydrodynamic systems** (like TUFLOW with Quadtree/Sub-Grid Sampling or 3Di) [7-9]. Alternatively, implement pre-simulated flood hazard map libraries (like the "City on Alert" system) that automatically trigger specific emergency protocols when measured or forecast rainfall/water levels hit predefined thresholds [10-12].
*   **Utilize Hybrid 1D/2D Fast Evaluation Models:** For urban pluvial flooding where stormwater drainage surcharges onto the surface, use hybrid models like the **Dynamic Cellular Automata Fast Flood Evaluation (Dynamic CA-ffé)**. These couple 1D stormwater network models (like SWMM) with 2D Cellular Automata surface models, providing reliable flood predictions in a matter of seconds to minutes [13, 14].

### 2. Hydrological Modelling Techniques
*   **Replace Cumulative Extreme Assumptions with Joint Probability:** In urban and coastal catchments, it is common to conservatively assume that maximum tide levels, peak streamflow, and peak local runoff all occur simultaneously. **Transition to joint probability methods based on observed data** [15-17]. Assuming cumulative maximums often leads to highly unrealistic "conservative" flood overlays that impose unnecessary legal, insurance, and administrative costs on properties [15, 16, 18].
*   **Adopt "Robustness" Metrics for Extreme Event Calibration:** When calibrating conceptual rainfall-runoff models for extreme events that exceed the historical record, standard objective functions often suffer from equifinality (where different parameter sets yield the same result). **Filter your parameter sets by selecting those with the lowest standard deviation across peak flows** (a robustness metric) to ensure more reliable hydrograph predictions for unseen, extreme storms [19-22].
*   **Enhance Rainfall Loss Estimation:** Relying purely on calibration to determine rainfall losses can lead to physically unrealistic values [23, 24]. Instead, **utilize spatially distributed rainfall loss maps derived from land cover and soil characteristics** to improve confidence in model parameters [23]. Furthermore, when conducting direct rainfall (rain-on-grid) modelling, pre-treat your digital elevation models to remove artificial trapped storage and utilize sub-grid sampling [25]. 

### 3. Case Studies and Extreme Events
*   **Rethink "Zero Afflux" and Over-Reliance on Structural Mitigation:** Strict adherence to "zero impact" (zero afflux) criteria in urban developments can create a false sense of security and a vicious cycle of levees and continued development [26, 27]. **Transition from mere hazard management (controlling flood elevations) to comprehensive risk mitigation** [26, 28]. This requires incorporating non-structural measures such as community education, strategic land-use planning, and improved early warning systems to build true community resilience [29, 30].
*   **Upgrade Flood Monitoring Infrastructure for Extreme Scenarios:** The 2023 Fitzroy River Bridge failure demonstrated that extreme floods cause dynamic, catastrophic changes to riverbeds, rendering traditional fixed-bed rating curves inaccurate and destroying floodplain gauges [31-33]. **Install image-based, AI-assisted streamflow cameras on high ground** to ensure data transmission continues when physical gauges fail, and utilize ground-penetrating radar for real-time scour monitoring [34-36].
*   **Account for Global Climate Drivers in Construction Planning:** Temporary drainage structures for construction projects are often designed for 1-in-2 or 1-in-10 AEP events, which are increasingly inadequate [37, 38]. **Assess global climate drivers before the construction phase**—specifically looking for a prevailing negative Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD), a positive Southern Annular Mode (SAM), or a La Niña coupled with a positive Southern Oscillation Index (SOI)—as these indicate a highly elevated risk of extreme, recurring rainfall that could wash out construction sites [39, 40].

### 4. Climate and Future Trends
*   **Abandon the Steady-State Yield Assumption:** Traditional water supply yield assessments assume a stationary climate, which will overestimate system yield and supply reliability as climates dry [41, 42]. **Incorporate dynamic simulation or covariate methods (like the Temperature Covariate Method)** into planning to accurately track the trajectory of drought risk and non-stationary demand [41-44].
*   **Design for Intensifying Sub-Hourly Extremes:** While daily rainfall totals may not show distinct trends in some regions, sub-hourly extreme rainfall is intensifying at alarming rates (up to 20% per decade in areas like Sydney) [45, 46]. Practitioners must **update urban flash-flood and stormwater infrastructure designs to accommodate these intensified short-duration bursts** [45, 47].
*   **Adjust Initial and Continuing Losses for Future Climates:** Do not assume historical loss parameters will remain static. Climate change is projected to increase both initial and continuing rainfall losses due to drier antecedent soil conditions [48, 49]. While these increased losses might offset the impact of larger rainfall events during frequent, minor floods, they will have minimal impact on rarer, extreme floods, increasing the variability of flood responses [49-51]. 
*   **Set "Climate-Ready" Environmental Flow Objectives:** Future environmental flows should **shift away from targeting specific historical species towards supporting "ecological functional groups"** (e.g., submerged vascular vegetation) that are more likely to adapt to changing conditions [52, 53]. When developing these flows, explicitly account for climate-driven reductions in groundwater recharge, which will severely impact critical low-flow periods [54, 55].
