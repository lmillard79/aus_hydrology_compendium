**Hydraulic Models and Parameters**
*   **Temporal Scale Dependency:** The impact of data resolution and modeling time steps on catchment behavior inference and model parameters remains poorly understood [1]. The current treatment of parameter scaling is largely empirical and heuristic, lacking an adequate mathematical framework [2]. Developing robust parameter scaling relationships requires numerous high-quality sets of very high temporal resolution data, which are currently unavailable for Australia [3]. 
*   **Loss and Gain Accounting:** In river system models, loss and gain accounting methods need further development to remain robust during validation. Future improvements rely on utilizing additional data, testing altered methods in more river reaches, and conducting site-specific investigations into why certain reaches underperform [4], [5]. 
*   **Limitations of 2D Depth-Averaged Models:** Current 2D numerical hydraulic models fail to accurately replicate complex and dynamic surface behaviors, such as cross-channel flow velocity and three-dimensional hydraulic jumps around obstacles [6], [7]. **Further study is required to quantify and model the impact of other floodplain obstacles (e.g., fences and cars) on urban flood flow behavior** [8].
*   **Uncertainty and Error Analysis:** There is a need to confirm qualitative model predictive error analysis methodologies in groundwater modeling by conducting "more similar experiments conducted on more complex, 'real-world' models" [9]. 
*   **Time-Varying Conceptual Parameters:** Using groundwater data to inform time-varying conceptual rainfall-runoff parameters shows promise, but **further investigation is required to determine exactly which parameters are best informed by groundwater levels**, and how structural improvements to models might influence this capability [10].

**Design Flood Estimation Techniques**
*   **Catchment Variability in Flood Models:** For the future revision of 2D flood emergency response tools, authors recommend explicitly taking into account "catchment variability," such as rainfall spatial and temporal patterns, rainfall intensity, and antecedent conditions [11]. 
*   **Joint Probability Frameworks:** The use of joint probability in estimating extreme sea levels and flood boundaries requires further research utilizing gauge data with a sufficiently long sample size to better calculate probability distribution parameters [12]. Similarly, **assumptions in joint probability models require further testing via hydraulic models using lower flows, shorter duration events, and duration-specific spatial patterns** [13], [14], [15].
*   **Non-Stationarity in Flood Frequency:** A significant gap exists in understanding the impact of climatic non-stationarity on the selection of probability distributions for flood frequency [16]. To address this, future updates to design guidelines should consider developing a separate set of Intensity-Frequency-Duration (IFD) estimates for high-risk modeling based purely on periods of elevated annual maxima [17].
*   **Large Flood Regionalisation Models (LFRM):** Future development of the LFRM is required to estimate flood quantiles up to the 2000-year Average Recurrence Interval (ARI), utilize variable *Ne* models to correct underestimation, derive uncertainty limits, and compare results to rainfall-runoff modeling [18]. 
*   **Temporal Patterns:** Peak flow estimates can be highly sensitive to the choice of temporal patterns; however, studies remain limited to single catchments. **Further research on input variability for a greater number of catchments is needed to draw firm conclusions** [19], [20].

**Software and Systems**
*   **Urban Water Design Scaling:** When combining local-scale tools (like MUSIC) with broader river system models (like eWater Source) for whole-of-system urban water design, further research into the appropriate scaling of parameters is required [21].
*   **Hydrology Education Tools:** A major gap exists in undergraduate teaching regarding conceptual modeling understanding and critical model evaluation. **There is an opportunity for the Australian hydrological community to collaborate on developing open-source, modular virtual tools to improve the teaching of hydrological simulation** [22], [23], [24].
*   **Software Enhancements:** For hydraulic software like MIKE (DHI), future versions need improved functionality for troubleshooting coupling issues, the ability to specify separate incoming/outgoing flow regulations, and better tools for catchpit modeling [25].
*   **Optimizing Environmental Water Deliveries:** When using optimization to inform the management of environmental water holdings, key areas for future improvement include representing sub-optimal and detrimental wet conditions in the objective function, testing frameworks in real catchments, and ensuring that river system model run times are not prohibitive [26].

**Water Supply and Demand Management**
*   **Non-Residential Urban Water Use:** While vast research exists on residential water demand, **there is a severe lack of models targeting non-residential urban water use** despite it comprising a significant portion of total demand [27], [28]. Future work must extend modeling to other user groups, evaluate predicted volumes over longer timeframes (e.g., 3 years), and incorporate climatic variables like temperature and rainfall [29].
*   **Decadal Climate Prediction:** Translating Global Climate Model (GCM) outputs into actionable water availability projections is difficult; **a lot more research is needed before rainfall and runoff can be predicted several years or decades ahead with sufficient skill to guide water resources planning** [30]. 
*   **Streamflow Forecasting via Catchment Wetness:** Utilizing catchment wetness for streamflow forecasting requires caution when applied to smaller catchments or those with small wetness stores relative to their size. Future forecasts could be improved by using dynamical catchment wetness models instead of relying solely on accumulated streamflow [31].

**Environmental and Specific Studies**
*   **Groundwater Dependent Ecosystems (GDEs):** In managing groundwater, such as in the Central Victorian Mineral Springs region, current maps do not confirm the presence of GDEs. **Further work is required to verify the exact locations of GDEs, identify their ecological value, and assess their specific water needs** [32].
*   **Ecological Responses to Consumptive Water Transfers:** To safely configure consumptive water transfers for ecological benefits, ongoing monitoring is needed to understand how flow pulses ("freshes") influence stream channel integrity, biological structure, and ecosystem function. This includes continuing investigations into developing diatom-based indices to assess water quality [33], [34], [35].
*   **Impact of Farm Dams:** There is considerable uncertainty in estimating the hydrological impact of stock and domestic farm dams. Researchers advise that current estimates should not be used to set policies on volumetric impacts without further improvements, such as utilizing ground surveys or LiDAR analysis to refine surface-area-to-volume relationships, and metering a representative sample of dams to better understand demand factors [36], [37]. 
*   **Salinity Management:** In studies of salinity in regions like Western Sydney, future work should involve additional analysis to identify climate trends associated with the time of borehole drilling and relating the estimated Standing Water Levels (SWLs) directly to the Australian Height Datum [38].
