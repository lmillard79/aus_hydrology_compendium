**Flood Modelling and Forecasting**

*   **Handling Extreme Events and Outliers:** A major gap in current data-driven forecasting is the performance of machine learning models during extreme events. For instance, while Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks excel at identifying temporal patterns, they struggle to predict hydrological extremes due to a scarcity of high-magnitude training data and a narrow focus on individual climate indices [1]. Similarly, Artificial Intelligence Neural Networks (AINN) sometimes produce skewed high-value outliers [2]. Future directions emphasize the development of **hybrid physics-informed models**, the integration of multiple climate indices, the use of transfer learning for data-scarce regions, and the incorporation of real-time satellite data [1].
*   **Refining Model Inputs and Parameters:** There is a strong need for improved handling of model inputs, particularly around **pre-burst rainfall handling** in lower-loss environments and optimal standard deviation values for Multiplicative Cascade Methods (MCM) [3]. In hydrodynamic modelling, researchers highlight the challenge of automated calibration; while well-established for semi-distributed hydrological models, calibrating complex hydrodynamic flood models remains highly sensitive and computationally demanding, requiring further methodological advancements [4].
*   **Improving 2D and Global Routing Models:** Practitioners face a lack of standardized guidance for representing structural features like bridges in 2D floodplain models, with significant work still required to improve accuracy and confidence in these representations [5, 6]. For large-scale modelling, tools like CaMa-Flood are currently constrained by coarse resolutions; future improvements should implement high-resolution local river networks (like the Australian Geofabric) and adjust parameters for channel width and bank height [7]. 
*   **Long-Range Forecasting:** There is currently a critical gap in operational long-range hydrological forecast services tailored for flood events [8]. Future development of long-range flood inundation outlooks will focus on improving the spatial accuracy of Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) maps via hydraulic modelling or machine learning, and expanding Bayesian Joint Probability (BJP) models across broader networks [9].

**Climate Change and Variability**

*   **Integrating Additional Climate Drivers:** Existing predictive models often focus narrowly on the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) and the Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD). Future research should expand predictor sets to include the **Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO)**, which could significantly improve streamflow forecasting accuracy [10]. Additionally, modelling architectures should be extended to explore lagged dual-mode relationships across different seasons [10], and analyses of the ENSO phase should be updated using the Bureau of Meteorology's new relative Niño index [11].
*   **Addressing Hydrologic and Saline Dynamics under Sea Level Rise:** The direction of change in catchment hydrology under climate change is highly uncertain. Future studies on estuarine saline dynamics recommend employing **probabilistic or Monte Carlo approaches** to account for this uncertainty [12, 13]. Furthermore, there is a lack of bathymetry data upstream of tidal limits; collecting this data is necessary to model how tidal limits might shift under sea-level rise and impact freshwater irrigators [14].
*   **Downscaling and Planning Uncertainties:** Machine learning techniques used for downscaling General Circulation Model (GCM) precipitation vary considerably in performance across different climatic settings; thus, suitability must be assessed on a case-by-case basis [15, 16]. For infrastructure planning, regulatory authorities face "decision-paralysis" due to the uncertainty in Shared-Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs). Researchers suggest exploring **defined temperature-rise trajectories** to provide a localized, adaptable approach to climate risk management [17, 18]. 

**Water Resource Management**

*   **Optimising Nature-based Solutions (NbS):** The spatial optimization of NbS across entire catchments remains underexplored, requiring **strategic micro-scale targeting** to place interventions where they deliver the greatest hydrological impact [19]. Future research should also lay the groundwork for multi-scalar optimization frameworks that incorporate both ecological indicators and socio-economic trade-offs [20]. To facilitate larger-scale adoption, there is a need for research focused on defining landscape flood capacities as **common property rights (or common pool resources)** [21].
*   **Urban Water Balances and Demand Forecasting:** Determining evidence-based assessments of net urban water balances is challenging due to existing data uncertainties. Future analyses require more reliable data on land use changes, impervious surface increases, water demands, and wastewater production [22]. In irrigation, demand models are limited by the assumption that all irrigators in a district behave identically; conceptual improvements are needed here, alongside the potential use of **real-time remote sensing to determine current crop areas** to reduce forecasting uncertainty [23, 24]. 
*   **Emerging Technologies:** There is a call to develop a forward-looking agenda to test pilot projects utilizing **quantum innovations** (sensing, computing, and communication) to accelerate water sustainability, though significant regulatory, social, and technological hurdles must first be navigated [25, 26].

**Community and Social Factors**

*   **Scaling Citizen Science Networks:** The "People's Catchment" project identified a strong need for extended research to engage and train citizen scientists in regional areas [27]. Future directions include testing and refining technologies—such as **low-cost automatic water-level sensors**—and developing robust data-sharing protocols so that community-generated data can be effectively integrated into official warning systems [28, 29].
*   **Enhancing Risk Communication:** Despite the availability of flood risk information, its translation for lay audiences remains poor [30]. Future studies will test current planning materials with planners, hydrologists, and community members to optimize how flood risk is communicated and understood [31].
*   **Transforming Water Governance:** To deal with institutional challenges in cross-border water governance, researchers recommend enhancing policy typologies to include the dynamics of narrative patterns over time [32]. A proposed future direction is modelling **"Territorial Intelligence,"** which frames the water basin as a cyber-physical system, to better understand the impact of rapidly integrating information and communication technologies (ICT) into governance and community participation [32].
