What are the key practical recommendations for floodplain managers, hydrologists, and water resources professionals? What actionable insights can practitioners apply immediately?

RESPONSE:
**Flood Estimation & Modelling**

*   **Integrate Hydrologic Pre-Processing with 2D Hydraulic Models:** Instead of relying entirely on computationally heavy hydraulic models, practitioners should use hydrologic models to pre-process flows [1]. Using hydrologic routing to identify critical storm events reduces the number of full hydrodynamic simulations required to map a catchment—sometimes from hundreds of thousands of runs down to just a few—saving significant time and cost [2-4]. 
*   **Adopt Direct Rainfall (Rain-on-Grid) with Sub-Grid Sampling (SGS):** 2D hydraulic models using direct rainfall are becoming a superior alternative to traditional lumped hydrologic models [5, 6]. Utilizing SGS allows the model to sample elevation data at a much finer resolution than the 2D cell size, which improves the definition of cell storage and conveyance, reduces cell-size sensitivity, and allows the use of larger cell sizes to significantly speed up run times without sacrificing accuracy [5, 7, 8].
*   **Apply Dynamically Varying Catchment Parameters:** Practitioners should move away from static parameters where physical conditions dictate otherwise. For instance, rather than altering loss parameters based on rainfall intensity, practitioners can dynamically vary catchment response times based on rainfall intensity while keeping loss parameters constant to achieve better calibration [9, 10]. Furthermore, hydraulic roughness for riparian vegetation (like Casuarinas) should not be treated as a fixed coefficient; vegetation bends and fails under different depth and velocity conditions, which significantly alters historic flood levels [11-13].
*   **Upgrade Floodway Design Practices:** Current 1D analysis techniques and broad-crested weir equations are often inadequate for floodway design [14, 15]. Practitioners should utilize 2D and 3D hydrodynamic modeling, particularly where both weir flow and orifice flow (e.g., blocked culverts) are present, to properly assess the hydraulic energy, scour potential, and necessary countermeasures [16, 17].

**Climate Change & Uncertainty**

*   **Shift to Scenario-Neutral Stress-Testing:** Traditional scenario-led assessments (relying on a few climate projections) can lead to decision paralysis due to uncertainty [18, 19]. Instead, practitioners should adopt a scenario-neutral approach. This involves identifying the specific climate attributes a water system is sensitive to, generating a broad range of plausible hydroclimatic futures (using stochastic weather generators), and stress-testing the system to identify exact failure modes and trigger points [20-22].
*   **Transition to an 'Operational Yield' Approach:** Static yield estimates evaluated at arbitrary future time-slices can be highly uncertain [23, 24]. Water planners should use an 'operational yield' approach that models system behavior starting from current storage conditions and steps forward progressively under future demand and climate projections [25-27]. This provides a realistic characterization of when a system will actually fail, directly informing the "time to action" for infrastructure investments [26, 27].
*   **Look Beyond the Median of Climate Projections:** Relying solely on the median ensemble of General Circulation Models (GCMs) can mask significant risks [28, 29]. Water security planners must assess the full 80% confidence interval (10th to 90th percentiles) of climate projections to establish precautionary, risk-informed adaptation strategies, especially where the consequences of supply shortfalls are severe [28, 30, 31].

**Urban Water Security**

*   **Implement Predictive Operational Models and Smart Sensors:** Water utilities can drastically reduce the likelihood of sewage spills and emergency relief structure discharges by deploying real-time data-driven sensors (e.g., Blokaid devices) [32, 33]. Combining this sensor data with predictive software running forecast spatial rainfall allows operators to anticipate pump station failures, pipe blockages, and system surcharges 4 to 6 hours in advance, allowing proactive resource allocation like dispatching eductor trucks before the spill occurs [34-37].
*   **Establish Collaborative Level of Service (LoS) Objectives:** Urban water security requires finding the balance between infrastructure cost, community needs, and the tolerance for water restrictions [38, 39]. Practitioners must clearly define LoS objectives in collaboration with the community and structure drought response strategies around optimizing existing sources, promoting fit-for-purpose water use, and timing the augmentation of resilient supplies (like desalination or recycled water) [40-42].

**Data Science in Hydrology**

*   **Deploy Machine Learning (ML) Surrogate Models for Early Warning:** Physically-based hydraulic models are often too slow for real-time flash flood forecasting. By training an ML surrogate model on the inputs and outputs of a calibrated hydrodynamic model, practitioners can predict complex urban flood depths and extents in a matter of seconds instead of hours [43-45]. 
*   **Utilize Mesh Modularization for Dynamic Environments:** A major limitation of ML surrogate models is that they must be entirely retrained whenever urban developments alter the landscape [46, 47]. Practitioners can solve this by "modularizing" the 2D finite volume solver's mesh. By isolating the contiguous subset of cells containing a terrain edit, only that module needs to be re-run and updated, maintaining surrogate accuracy while minimizing computational costs [48-50].
*   **Apply Bayesian Joint Probability (BJP) for Demand Forecasting:** River operators can replace the assumption that ordered water equals demanded water with BJP statistical models. These models predict daily water demands and their uncertainties in real-time based on orders, reducing the risk of over-releasing (causing downstream flooding) or under-releasing (causing supply shortfalls) [51, 52].
*   **Adopt Hybrid Spatial Rainfall Methods:** To combat data sparsity and scarcity in operational flow forecasting, practitioners should use hybrid algorithms to calculate hourly spatial rainfall. By dynamically disaggregating gridded daily rain using localized gauge patterns or Thiessen weighted averages, models can maintain high availability and resist data interference/missing records, leading to significantly lower bias in continuous forecasting [53-55].
