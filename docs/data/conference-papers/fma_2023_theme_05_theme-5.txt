While maps are a foundational tool for determining flood constraints such as inundation extents, flow conveyance, and peak hazard levels, they only **"tell part of the story of flood risk"** [1]. Managing flooding "beyond maps" requires recognizing that a static map cannot be used in isolation to inform complex decisions; it must be combined with technical advice, an understanding of compounding vulnerabilities, and risk-informed strategies [1-3]. 

Within this broader context, the sources identify several Best Practice Solutions for modern floodplain management:

**1. Transitioning to Real-Time Decision Support Systems**
Static maps are often too slow and theoretical for dynamic emergency response [4, 5]. Best practice involves shifting toward real-time Decision Support Systems (DSS) and continuous flood intelligence that deliver **"the right information, in the right format, to the right people, at the right time"** [6-8]. By integrating rainfall nowcasting, gauge telemetry, and machine learning (ML) algorithms, authorities can rapidly forecast flood depths and extents for incoming storms [5, 9-11]. This allows disaster management to become preventative rather than reactive, providing critical lead time to protect assets and coordinate evacuations [5, 11].

**2. Empowering the Community with Usable Data**
Flood mapping is only effective if the public can understand and act upon it [12, 13]. Traditional hydraulic maps can be technically intimidating, so best practice solutions focus on **translating complex data into intuitive, community-facing formats** [14, 15]. Examples include:
*   **Interactive Public Portals:** Dashboards that link local river gauges to specific property risks, allowing residents to view estimated water depths and flood intelligence cards rather than just a blue polygon on a map [16-20].
*   **Advanced Visualizations:** The use of 3D mapping and Augmented Reality (AR) to overlay predicted flood levels directly onto Google Street View images of local homes, helping residents visualize the true, physical threat [21, 22].
*   **Proactive Education:** Initiatives that take flood studies "off the planning shelf and into the classroom" via specialized lesson plans, ensuring that the next generation understands their local catchment and flood safety profiles [23, 24].

**3. Integrating Emergency and Floodplain Management**
Best practice dictates that Flood Risk Management (FRM) and Emergency Management (EM) must operate as a unified system [25, 26]. **Flood emergency plans must be risk-based, scalable, and adaptable to all forms of flooding** [27]. This requires a "Total Flood Warning System" that interprets flood data to outline specific consequences—such as road closures or isolated communities—based on real-time stream heights, enabling a coordinated, whole-of-community response [27-29].

**4. Implementing a Holistic Suite of Mitigation Strategies**
Because maps reveal that it is impossible to eliminate all flood risk, managers must adopt a diverse portfolio of mitigation measures suited to specific local conditions [30, 31]:
*   **Nature-Based Solutions (NbS):** Moving away from exclusively relying on conventional engineering, best practice increasingly incorporates NbS to "make room for the river." Solutions such as restoring wetlands, reconnecting floodplains, and reforestation help to naturally detain, reduce, and divert floodwaters [32-34]. Crucially, NbS build long-term landscape resilience and offer co-benefits like carbon sequestration, water quality improvement, and biodiversity conservation [33-36].
*   **Robust Structural Interventions:** Where infrastructure is built, it requires rigorous civil and geotechnical design because "WATER IS POWERFUL," and unmaintained levees or bridge abutments frequently fail during major events [37, 38]. Where legacy planning has left properties highly exposed, custom flood barriers are recognized as a valid risk-reduction tool, though they are not a substitute for keeping development out of the floodplain [39-41].
*   **Non-Structural Measures:** In areas where physical mitigation or levees are not economically or practically viable, best practice relies on strict land-use planning controls, community flood readiness, and voluntary purchase (buyback) programs to permanently remove residents from high-hazard zones [42, 43].
