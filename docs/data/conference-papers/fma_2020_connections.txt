RESPONSE:
The research themes of Scheme Types, Eligibility Criteria, Success Factors, and Lessons Learnt in property-based floodplain management are deeply interconnected, united by the central tension between technical engineering requirements and complex socio-economic realities. 

**Connecting the Main Themes**

*   **Scheme Types dictate Eligibility Criteria:** The specific type of property-based scheme directly determines the technical eligibility criteria required for participation. For example, Voluntary House Purchase (VHP) schemes typically target properties in high-hazard floodways where there is a significant risk to life [1]. In contrast, house resilience schemes, such as voluntary house raising, are generally excluded from floodways and limited to low-hazard areas [2]. Furthermore, the funding source for the scheme type can dictate criteria; for instance, USA FEMA-funded VHP schemes require a property to have a history of repetitive insured losses and a positive benefit-cost ratio [3].
*   **Eligibility Criteria intersect with Lessons Learnt and Success Factors:** A major **lesson learnt** is that rigidly applying technical eligibility criteria can cause significant social distress, such as splitting up families or dividing tight-knit communities [4], [5]. Consequently, a critical **success factor** is the willingness to relax these criteria for social reasons. For example, local governments have achieved better scheme uptake by purchasing adjacent properties that do not technically meet the flood criteria, ensuring that neighbors and families can relocate together [3], [4], [6]. Additionally, another lesson learnt is that eligibility criteria must be kept simple and clearly communicated to the public to prevent misconceptions and build trust [7], [8].
*   **Lessons Learnt drive Success Factors:** The challenges encountered in past schemes directly inform best practices. Recognizing the lesson that the elderly find it particularly difficult to relocate due to long-standing connections to the land [5], [8], successful schemes now prioritize providing homeowners with flexible options (e.g., offering a choice between land-swaps, purchase, or house raising) and assisting them with "considered relocation" to nearby areas using GIS to keep communities intact [9], [6]. Furthermore, the lesson that voluntary property acquisition takes years to complete is offset by the success factor of using recent disaster events as an impetus, capitalizing on residents' willingness to move immediately following a flood [6], [8].

**Cross-Cutting Issues and Interdisciplinary Connections**

*   **Engineering vs. Social Psychology:** Property-based management bridges hydrology and human psychology. While engineers use models to determine flood hazard and scheme viability, the implementation relies heavily on community engagement and psychological support. Residents are often dealing with high levels of post-disaster distress, generational ties to the land, and trauma [5], [10]. Successful schemes require an interdisciplinary team with mediation and facilitation skills, rather than just technical expertise, to guide residents through the emotionally taxing process of relocation [10], [6].
*   **Urban Planning, Economics, and Real Estate:** Property schemes frequently intersect with macro-economic and real estate factors. A key lesson learnt is that local housing booms can destroy a project's budget and price homeowners out of buying a new property in the same area [11], [8]. Furthermore, from an urban planning perspective, governments face the challenge of lost tax revenue when residents leave a district [12], [8], and must carefully plan a beneficial, income-generating, or recreational use for the vacated floodplain land to justify the expenditure of public funds [13], [8].

**Specific Examples of Papers Bridging Multiple Themes**

*   **A. Mosely (Jacobs) - *Purchase or Move? Lessons Learnt from Historic Property-Based Floodplain Management Schemes*:** This is the foundational paper that bridges all four themes [14]. Mosely analyzes global and Australian case studies (such as Grantham, Queensland, and Rhineland, Missouri) to demonstrate how **Scheme Types** (like the Lockyer Valley land-swap [15]) rely on strict **Eligibility Criteria** (e.g., suffering devastating loss of life and property [10]), but ultimately succeed or fail based on **Success Factors** like strong local leadership and the **Lesson Learnt** that relocated communities must be kept together [10], [9], [6].
*   **S. Yeo et al. (Infrastructure NSW) - *Both Flood and Community Engagement Specialists Working Together...*:** This paper bridges the engineering of flood mapping with the **Success Factor** of community engagement [16]. It reinforces Mosely's finding that technical criteria must be communicated simply [7]. Yeo et al. detail how the Hawkesbury-Nepean Valley strategy paired flood engineers with communication experts to translate complex flood data into accessible, plain-English tools (like interactive maps and animations) to build community resilience and understanding [17], [18].
*   **J. Nadiroglu et al. (BG&E) - *The Only Way Is Up – Flood Evacuation in Parramatta CBD*:** This paper bridges **Scheme Types** (specifically building resilience and vertical evacuation) with **Eligibility Criteria** (high hazard areas). While Mosely discusses retreating from the floodplain via house purchases [19], Nadiroglu explores the alternative for high-density, high-hazard areas like the Parramatta CBD where retreat isn't viable. Due to flash flooding and H5 hazard levels outside, the building's design criteria force a "shelter-in-place" approach, integrating automatic flood barriers and upper-level refuge areas as the ultimate property-based management strategy [20], [21], [22].
