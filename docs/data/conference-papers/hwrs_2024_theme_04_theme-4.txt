**Hydrological Non-Stationarity and Catchment Resilience**
A dominant theme at HWRS 2024 is the recognition that **historical assumptions of a stationary climate are no longer valid for hydrological modelling** [1, 2]. Events like the Millennium Drought have triggered persistent shifts in rainfall-runoff relationships, a phenomenon where a given amount of rainfall produces significantly less streamflow today than it did in previous decades [3, 4]. Researchers have found strong evidence that **catchments exhibit finite resilience and may possess multiple co-existent steady states**, meaning that once pushed into a low-runoff state by severe drought, they may not recover even when normal rainfall returns [4-6]. While the exact causes remain complex, this non-stationarity is driven by compounding factors including climate change, shifting vegetation dynamics (such as increased plant water use and carbon dioxide fertilization effects), groundwater decline, and human interventions like farm dams [7-10]. 

**Evolving Flood Dynamics and Design Flood Estimation**
Climate change is fundamentally altering flood risks through two competing mechanisms: **drier antecedent catchment conditions and intensifying extreme rainfalls** [11, 12]. Higher global temperatures increase the atmosphere's moisture-holding capacity, leading to more intense precipitation; short-duration extreme rainfalls in Australia are projected to increase by a median of 15% per degree of warming, while long-duration extremes increase by 8% per degree [13-15]. **For frequent, smaller floods, drier soil conditions act as a buffer, increasing rainfall losses and dampening flood peaks** [11, 12]. However, **for rare, severe floods, the intensification of extreme rainfall overwhelms the drier soils, leading to significant increases in flood magnitudes** [12]. 

To address this, the recent update to the Climate Change Considerations chapter in Australian Rainfall and Runoff (ARR) provides new mandates for design flood estimation [16-18]. Practitioners must now adjust Intensity-Frequency-Duration (IFD) data and losses to account for the warming that has already occurred since the 1961-1990 baseline, which significantly increases existing condition flood levels [16]. Furthermore, research highlights that **the critical duration of storm events is generally shortening under future climate scenarios**, meaning catchments will respond more rapidly and intensely to shorter, heavier bursts of rain [19-21]. Tools like the newly developed Climate Change Calculator (CCC) are being deployed to help practitioners quickly estimate how flood probabilities will shift under different Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) without requiring computationally expensive re-modelling [22, 23].

**Adaptive Water Resource Management and Decision-Making**
Because future climate projections carry deep uncertainty, traditional "top-down" modelling approaches often lead to decision paralysis [24-26]. HWRS 2024 highlights a shift towards **"bottom-up" or "scenario-neutral" stress-testing frameworks** [27, 28]. These approaches test a water system's vulnerability across a wide matrix of plausible hydroclimatic changes to identify the exact thresholds that cause system failure, allowing managers to better evaluate adaptation options [27, 28].

To effectively communicate these complex risks, planners are adopting the **"Storylines Approach"**, which translates vast arrays of climate data into physically self-consistent, plausible future narratives (e.g., an "Optimistic" slightly drier future vs. a "Conservative" significantly warmer and drier future) [29-32]. This was successfully applied to Perth's Integrated Water Supply Scheme to help asset managers plan for future inflows [33, 34]. Additionally, **adaptive reservoir operation policies optimized through multi-objective frameworks** are being proposed as an alternative to expensive and disruptive infrastructure expansions, allowing existing systems to dynamically respond to lower water availability and higher demands [35-37].

**Advancements in Hydrological Modelling**
To keep pace with these climate challenges, hydro-informatics is rapidly advancing. Researchers are increasingly **co-developing Process-Based Models (PBMs) with Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Deep Learning**, such as using Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to improve streamflow routing and forecasting in large, complex land surface models [38, 39]. Models are also being structurally adapted to account for non-stationarity, such as explicitly linking actual evapotranspiration to satellite-derived Leaf Area Index (LAI) to capture the effects of changing vegetation dynamics on future streamflows [9, 40]. Furthermore, **ensemble forecasting systems**—which provide hundreds of probabilistic flow scenarios rather than a single deterministic prediction—are becoming standard operational practice to better quantify the deep uncertainties associated with extreme droughts and floods [41-43].
