The research themes within this notebook are deeply interconnected, illustrating a paradigm shift in floodplain management: moving away from static, purely theoretical risk models toward dynamic, real-time, and community-contextualised flood intelligence. 

Here are the cross-cutting issues and interdisciplinary connections across the main themes:

**1. From Static Maps to Dynamic Reality (Connecting Mappable Constraints, Flood Mapping Limitations, and Factors Beyond Maps)**
While **Mappable Constraints** are foundational for identifying flood extents, flow conveyance, and hazard classifications [1], [2], [3], researchers emphasize significant **Flood Mapping Limitations**. A map is merely an indicator of risk and cannot be used in isolation to inform comprehensive decision-making [4], [5]. The literature bridges these themes by introducing **Factors Beyond Maps**, noting that real-world flood risk is heavily influenced by dynamic variables that are difficult to plot on a standard 2D map. These include the condition of mitigation infrastructure, disruptions to community services (like power and communications), antecedent catchment conditions, and effective warning times [6], [7], [8]. Furthermore, presenting static maps without context can confuse the public, as community members often struggle to interpret technical data like annual exceedance probabilities or complex velocity metrics [9], [10].

**2. Reframing the "Unprecedented" through Intelligence (Connecting Unprecedented vs Unplanned with Warning Infrastructure)**
The theme of **Unprecedented vs Unplanned** directly challenges the media's frequent use of terms like "rain bomb" or "unprecedented" to describe major floods [11], [12]. Research argues that events are usually labeled "unprecedented" not simply because of their hydrological size, but because they exceed the existing capability, capacity, and emergency planning of responding agencies [13], [14]. This directly intersects with **Warning Infrastructure**. By transitioning from reactive responses to proactive ones using real-time flood intelligence, authorities can prevent an event from becoming "unprecedented" by better coordinating resources and evacuations ahead of time [15], [16]. 

**3. Bridging the Gap with Technology and Communication (Connecting Flood Mapping Limitations, Best Practice Solutions, and Warning Infrastructure)**
**Best Practice Solutions** increasingly rely on advanced **Warning Infrastructure** to overcome **Flood Mapping Limitations**. Traditional physics-based 2D hydrodynamic models, while excellent for long-term planning, are computationally intensive and far too slow to be useful in a rapidly evolving emergency [17], [18]. To solve this, hydrology is intersecting with data science. Researchers are deploying Machine Learning (ML) models and digital twins that can ingest live data streams to generate highly accurate, scalable flood forecasts in seconds rather than hours [19], [20]. Concurrently, social sciences and communication frameworks, such as the Data Information Knowledge Wisdom (DIKW) pyramid, are being employed to translate this complex technical data into meaningful, location-specific warnings that empower the community to act [21], [22].

**Specific Examples of Papers Bridging Multiple Themes:**

*   **N. Bichel's "Unprecedented or Unplanned" [13]:** This paper bridges **Unprecedented vs Unplanned**, **Warning Infrastructure**, and **Best Practice Solutions**. It argues that the point where an event becomes "unprecedented" is directly relative to the planning and response actions of agencies [14]. It demonstrates how integrating dynamic flood intelligence into dashboards—such as the Transport for NSW TECCH dashboard—transforms flood risk management by providing real-time situational awareness that keeps transport networks safe [23], [24].
*   **M. Boshoff & J. Duncan's "Is data just numbers without context?" [25]:** This paper bridges **Flood Mapping Limitations**, **Mappable Constraints**, and **Best Practice Solutions**. It addresses the modern challenge of communicating highly complex, multi-variable flood performance criteria to the public [26], [27]. The authors utilize the DIKW model and automated GIS analysis techniques to move beyond confusing, binary "exceedance" maps, aiming to present flood risk in a digestible format that gives communities true context [21], [28], [22].
*   **S. Ho & M. Holcomb's "The Future of Flood Prediction and Warning: Machine Learning and Digital Twin" [29]:** This research bridges **Flood Mapping Limitations** and **Warning Infrastructure**. It contrasts the computational limits of traditional hydraulic modeling with the agility of Machine Learning [17], [30]. By highlighting a case study in Bangkok, it shows how hybrid solutions can predict local flows within a 6-hour window in under a second, effectively bridging the gap between theoretical modeling and real-time emergency response [31], [32].
*   **A. Toniato & D. McLuckie's "Managing Flooding Beyond Maps" [33]:** This paper directly bridges **Mappable Constraints** and **Factors Beyond Maps**. It cautions that while mapping hazard categories is a necessary step, the maps only tell part of the story [5]. It advocates for holistic decision-making that incorporates unmappable human and environmental factors, such as flood readiness, evacuation problems, and natural variability [34], [8].
