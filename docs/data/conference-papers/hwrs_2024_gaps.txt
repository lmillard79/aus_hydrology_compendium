**Extreme Flood Events**
**Understanding and modelling rare floods** presents a significant knowledge gap, as current methods carry considerable uncertainty when dealing with extreme events [1, 2]. Researchers strongly recommend incorporating **multiple lines of evidence—such as palaeoflood data, indigenous oral accounts, and documented histories**—to build greater confidence in the frequency and magnitude of rare floods [1, 3-5]. 

Following the record-breaking 2022 floods in Eastern Australia, authors identified a critical need to **move beyond simple event-based modelling** to mixed continuous/event-based approaches that can better capture the complex interaction between initial soil wetness conditions and extreme rainfall [6, 7]. Furthermore, **multi-peak streamflow events and significantly reduced flood propagation times** caught communities by surprise, highlighting a research gap in incorporating these phenomena into design hydrographs and emergency evacuation procedures [8, 9].

In terms of parameter reliability, studies indicate that **more case studies across diverse geographic locations are needed** to validate nonlinearity parameters for hydrologic routing [10, 11] and to better understand low-flow outliers in Flood Frequency Analysis (FFA) datasets [12]. 

There is also a lack of empirical data in Australia regarding **building structural vulnerability to dynamic flood loading**, indicating a need for future research to adapt international literature to local conditions [13, 14]. Additionally, the industry requires **collaboration with approval authorities to holistically define acceptable "afflux"** (changes in flood levels) and risk tolerances, accounting for cumulative impacts, hazard changes, and climate change [15-17].

**Renewable Energy Infrastructure**
Flood modelling for renewable energy sites (like solar farms, wind farms, and battery storage) is hampered by **severe data scarcity in remote areas** [18, 19]. Missing information includes reliable initial and continuing loss (IL/CL) values, high-resolution LiDAR, and detailed river discharge records [19, 20]. Because these projects often sit in large upstream catchments with dispersed structural assets, modellers must currently rely on blending high and low-resolution Digital Elevation Models and applying computationally intensive Rain-on-Grid techniques [20, 21]. 

For solar farms specifically, **field-based ecohydrologic research is extremely limited and lacks validation against computational modelling** [22-24]. Authors emphasize the need for field studies on **slopes greater than 5% or 8%**, where sheet flow and erosion risks escalate [24]. A major regulatory gap is that **current construction general permits are inadequate for the unique design of solar farms**, and there is a lack of guidance on solar-specific stormwater management strategies that prioritize infiltration over traditional impervious disconnection [25, 26]. Future frameworks should explore concepts like the "food-energy-water" nexus or "techno-ecological synergy" to ensure sustainable development [26].

**Stormwater & Dam Analytics**
**Dam breach modelling** relies heavily on non-deterministic methodologies and older regression equations from limited datasets, generating substantial uncertainty in peak outflow and consequence estimates [27, 28]. Future research should **explore additional modern regression equations (e.g., USBR, Azmi Thomson), alternative hydraulic analysis tools (like TUFLOW, HEC-RAS 2D, or McBREACH), and validate outcomes across more dam locations** including both flood failure and sunny-day piping failures [29, 30].

To accurately assess the consequences of a dam failure, the **Stochastic Storm Transposition (SST) method requires improvement for frequent storms**, as the commonly used bivariate log-normal approach drastically underestimates coincident rainfall variability [31, 32].

Regarding hydraulic structures like spillways and stilling basins, current **experimental instrumentation cannot continuously measure internal air-water flow properties** (such as bubble size or internal velocities) in three dimensions [33, 34]. Closing this gap requires developing advanced phase-detection probes and utilizing **remote sensing (LIDAR and drones) to gather quantitative, prototype-scale validation data** [35-37].

In urban stormwater systems, there is a recognized need to **apply Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) to better understand the complex, dynamic nature of sewer catchment hydrology**, including inflow and infiltration [38-40]. Furthermore, researchers recommend investigating the specific mechanisms of **how changing rainfall and potential evapotranspiration (PET) will impact Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD) treatment devices** to create climate-resilient infrastructure [41].

**Climate Change & Hydrology**
A major paradox identified in the literature is **hydrological non-stationarity and streamflow non-recovery following prolonged droughts** [42, 43]. Catchments are shifting to lower runoff states per unit of rainfall, but the precise biophysical causes remain unknown [42, 44]. Future modelling needs to **investigate biophysical drivers like Soil Moisture Storage Capacity, multi-decadal groundwater dynamics, and land cover clearance** to understand this phenomenon [45-48]. Researchers also note that assessing climate impacts on rainfall-partitioning will require moving away from streamflow-only tests to "big laboratories" (large-scale virtual or physical catchment experiments) [49].

The **complex interactions between vegetation dynamics and hydrology require much deeper exploration** [50]. Specifically, the **effects of carbon dioxide fertilization on plant water use efficiency** and regional runoff are highly uncertain, making it difficult to upscale laboratory findings to catchment-level projections [51]. A related gap is how **actual evapotranspiration (AET)** will change in the future, and how to tractably model these changes using lumped conceptual rainfall-runoff models in operational settings [52, 53].

While the water quantity impacts of climate change are widely studied, **the impact of longer dry spells on urban water quality and pollutant concentrations is heavily under-researched** [54]. 

Finally, **the impact of climate change on design flood estimation parameters requires further investigation** [55]. Researchers suggest that future work should move toward gridded or localized Areal Reduction Factors (ARFs) and rerun loss calibrations using data that better captures specific storm mechanisms, such as East Coast Lows [56, 57].
