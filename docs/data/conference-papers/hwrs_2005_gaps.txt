RESPONSE:
The research presented at the 2005 Hydrology and Water Resources Symposium highlights several critical gaps, unanswered questions, and avenues for future research across diverse water management themes. 

**Hydrologic Sensitivity**
*   **Climate Change Assumptions:** When assessing hydrologic sensitivity to climate change, current models often scale historical rainfall series, which assumes that the distribution and timing of rainfall remain unchanged. However, because extreme rainfall events are expected to increase in a greenhouse-enhanced climate, future research needs to account for changes in rainfall intensity, as scaling methods may under- or over-estimate runoff sensitivities depending on the region [1]. 
*   **Evapotranspiration Parameters:** Water balance models have been found to be relatively insensitive to most parameters except those associated with evapotranspiration, suggesting that future monitoring of the implied proportion of rainfall required to balance evapotranspiration is needed to provide better parameter bounds [2]. Furthermore, when comparing one- and two-source energy balance models for estimating actual evapotranspiration via remote sensing, large discrepancies exist over dry, bare soil conditions. Further investigations are required to understand these differences and determine which model is most reliable [3, 4].
*   **Groundwater-River Interactions:** While hydraulic connections between rivers and aquifers have been mapped, a major research gap remains in quantifying the temporal dynamics of water fluxes. More detailed analyses of groundwater extraction data are needed to understand the relationship between reduced groundwater levels and reduced baseflow events. Consequently, future research must focus on developing simple, integrated groundwater-river models that capture the key processes driving these interactions [5]. The long-term modeling of these interactions is technically complex and current management approaches require major technical assumptions and significant judgement [6]. 
*   **Soft Sulfidic Soils:** The unconfined groundwater hydrology of gel-like, soft sulfidic soils requires more quantitative testing to confirm qualitative observations. Specifically, the impact of electrolyte concentrations on the hydraulic properties and consolidation of these materials is a key area requiring further research [7, 8].

**Stream Physical Conditions**
*   **Hydraulic Habitat Assessment:** While reach-scale dimensionless hydraulic parameters (such as the Froude number) are useful for assessing in-stream heterogeneity, further work is needed to define their natural temporal and spatial variability across different geomorphic regions [9]. Classifications for visual cross-sectional flow-typing also need refinement; researchers propose combining "run" and "riffle" biotopes into a single "rippled flow" category because hydraulic parameters failed to detect a significant difference between them [10]. Future rapid surveys should also note the presence or absence of rarer features (like cascades) to avoid missing important ecological information [11]. 
*   **Ecological Responses and Biological Monitoring:** Current sampling methodologies for macroinvertebrates downstream of wastewater plants are highly variable; further analysis is needed to establish how many samples must be collected to accurately represent faunal assemblages and monitor effluent impacts [12, 13]. Additionally, ecological modeling should not rely solely on modeled discharge data; future research must gather synchronous hydrological field data, as modeled data often fails to adequately capture smaller, ecologically significant flow events [14, 15].
*   **Pool Scour and Environmental Flows:** Traditional engineering models predicting that flows will scour out deep, long pools around large woody debris have proven inadequate, as local variability and debris buildup complicate the process. Future approaches must mesh long-term hydrology with hydraulics and biology to consider flow-geomorphology interactions in a biologically meaningful way [16, 17]. Furthermore, environmental flow planning needs to be expanded to incorporate system dynamics during extreme and prolonged drought conditions, ensuring water can be allocated equitably [18, 19].
*   **Channel Erosion:** When analyzing historical river channel changes using GIS, the influence of past climatic conditions (e.g., the 1860s) on the initiation of erosion remains an unanswered question slated for future research, alongside the planned evaluation of an additional 20 lowland rivers [20, 21].

**Modelling and Simulation**
*   **Model Selection and Uncertainty:** A significant gap identified in the Catchment Modelling Toolkit is the lack of a structured system or rule-base to help users select the most appropriate model for a specific problem. Future developments aim to gather case study information in a consistent way to support data mining, detailing model uncertainty, parameter sensitivity, and the specific circumstances each model is suited for [22-24]. 
*   **Model Enhancements:** Several simulation models have slated improvements. For the IHACRES rainfall-runoff model, planned enhancements include a Catchment Moisture Deficit (CMD) non-linear module, baseflow filtering tools, spectral analysis tools, and sensitivity analysis capabilities [25]. For water entitlement modeling, a major weakness is the spatial distribution of runoff; future iterations should use GIS to incorporate different land use patterns and soil types into runoff calculations, as well as model variable demand patterns, priority-based extractions, and return flows [26, 27].
*   **Stochastic and Probabilistic Modelling:** In applying the Hierarchical Mixture of Experts (HME) framework, the primary challenge for future applications in gauged and ungauged catchments is determining the appropriate mechanism (and choosing the right predictor variables) to trigger the switch from one catchment state model to another [28, 29]. For multi-site stochastic data generation, future research will investigate applying the HMM/AR(1) framework to various sites around Australia [30, 31]. Additionally, for estuarine flood level modeling, future work must focus on preserving temporal correlation in daily spatial rainfall disaggregation models and generating cross-correlated weather forcing sequences at sub-daily time scales [32].
*   **Calibration Errors:** Research into Catchment Modelling Systems (CMS) revealed that using an AR(1) model to remove autocorrelation from model errors—followed by applying a Mean Squared Error (MSE) objective function—resulted in extremely poor predictive capacity, indicating that this method of calibration needs severe reevaluation [33]. 
*   **Applying UMCST to Large Catchments:** The URBS-Monte Carlo Simulation Technique (UMCST) showed large discrepancies between derived flood frequency curves and observed data in certain catchments (e.g., South Johnstone River). Future investigations must determine if this is due to inappropriate specification of input variables, calibration errors, or faulty streamflow data [34]. Routine application of UMCST will also require the regionalization of input variables [35].

**Climate and Storage**
*   **General Circulation Models (GCMs):** Current methodologies using GCMs to generate fine-scale rainfall scenarios suffer from high sampling variability. Future research will apply these approaches to multimodel ensembles of GCMs and emission scenarios to establish a risk-based probabilistic approach. Strategies like smoothing with adjacent months or adjacent grid squares also require further evaluation to reduce sampling variability [36, 37]. 
*   **Design Rainfall and IFD Estimation:** Methods for deriving Intensity-Frequency-Duration (IFD) design rainfalls lack confidence intervals; future research expects to use resampling techniques to derive these intervals for specific locations and durations [38]. Additionally, accurately quantifying how climate change will impact the frequency and intensity of extreme rainfalls remains difficult, though qualitative assessments are identified as a necessary future guidance tool for engineers [39, 40].
*   **Flood Data Coverage:** Studies analyzing the multidecadal variability of flood risks are currently limited by geographic coverage. More data with larger spatial coverage and varying catchment sizes are needed to definitively identify which areas are most influenced by Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation (IPO) modulation [41]. 

**Urban Hydrology**
*   **Constructed Stormwater Wetlands:** While dissolved nitrogen dominates urban runoff, current wetland designs often focus on particulate removal. Further research is heavily needed to understand the processes governing the transformation of bio-available dissolved nitrogen (DON, NOx, and NH3) so wetlands can be optimized to treat it [42, 43]. For modeling sediment removal in wetlands, ongoing work aims to test newly developed equations across multiple wetlands without further calibration, with the ultimate goal of extending the approach to predict the removal of particle-bound pollutants like total phosphorus and heavy metals [44].
*   **Pervious Pavements:** Research on pervious pavements is still in its initial stages in Australia. Prior to industry acceptance, more laboratory and field tests are required to develop a comprehensive design framework that optimizes load-bearing capacity, long-term performance (avoiding clogging), and the quality of infiltrating water [45, 46].
*   **Areal Reduction Factors (ARFs):** When applying ARFs to estimate flood peaks in urban catchments, using a uniform factor based on the overall catchment area can underestimate peak flows. Future studies need to undertake detailed combined hydrological and hydraulic analyses of tributary catchments, considering various storm return periods (e.g., 5, 20, 50-year ARI), to exactly define the boundaries governing changes in critical storm durations [47, 48].
