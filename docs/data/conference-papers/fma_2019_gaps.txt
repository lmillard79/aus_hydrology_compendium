RESPONSE:
Here are the research gaps, future directions, and unanswered questions identified in the provided papers, categorized by the themes in your query:

**Bureau of Meteorology (BoM) and Forecasting**
*   **Quantifying Uncertainty and Ensemble Prediction:** A major future direction for flood forecasting is reducing and quantifying uncertainty using ensemble prediction (which samples a range of initial conditions and parameters) and statistical post-processing [1], [2]. Modellers also need to determine how to effectively translate this predictive uncertainty into emergency response actions [3], [4].
*   **Big Data and AI:** There are unanswered questions regarding how artificial intelligence and big data can be used to combine modelled outputs, historical data, local intelligence, and social media for rapid warnings during fast-evolving floods [5].
*   **Non-stationarity and Ungauged Basins:** Research is needed to incorporate non-stationarity into modelling (due to changing land use and climate change) and to develop better coverage for ungauged basins using platforms like SMART [5]. 
*   **Climate-Informed Hydrology:** Current models often rely on traditional random/independent assumptions. Researchers identify a gap in understanding the non-randomness of hydrologic events, suggesting a move toward a "climate-informed hydrology paradigm" that incorporates macro-climatic factors (like the Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation) to predict bounded irregularities in extreme floods [6], [7].

**Social Impact Analysis**
*   **Psycho-Social Behaviour:** Significant research gaps exist regarding the psychology of risk aversion. Questions remain about what factors lead to the cognitive dissonance and "optimism bias" that encourage illogical behaviour during floods (such as driving into floodwater), and how to reduce community dependency on emergency authorities [8], [9], [10].
*   **Health Impacts and Secondary Stressors:** More investigation is needed into the long-term physical and mental health impacts of flooding across different demographics, and how secondary stressors (like delayed insurance claims and disaster relief) can be reduced [11].
*   **Comprehensive Social Assessment:** The social impacts of Floodplain Risk Management (FRM) options are rarely fully assessed. Current studies often fail to evaluate the **"levee paradox"** (where levees increase risk-taking and reduce preparedness), visual amenity impacts, social capital dislocation, and equity issues [12], [13].

**Economic Assessment Framework**
*   **Monetizing Intangible Losses:** Current Cost-Benefit Analyses (CBAs) for flood projects are highly conservative because they primarily focus on tangible property damage [14], [15]. A critical research gap is the lack of "rapid" assessment techniques to reliably quantify intangible damages in dollar terms, such as risk to life, mental health impacts, and social disruption [15], [16], [17].
*   **Future-Climate Risks and Compounding Perils:** Existing funding evaluations do not adequately capture the present value of mitigating future-climate risks. There is a need to understand the compounding economic risk in areas exposed to multiple climate-driven perils (e.g., floods overlapping with severe bushfires or cyclones) [18], [19].
*   **Macro-Financial Impacts:** Unanswered questions remain about how to quantify whole-of-flood costs, the whole-of-life costs of resilient buildings, and how to effectively engage large investors in flood prevention and recovery [20].

**Data and Quality Assurance**
*   **The "Big Flood Data" Problem:** As models advance from 1D to high-resolution 2D, the size of output data is growing exponentially. Agencies face a major gap in managing this data, requiring better frameworks for version control, rollback, data discovery, and preventing the loss of corporate knowledge [21], [22], [23].
*   **Raw vs. Quality-Controlled Data:** There is a critical tension between the high demand for near real-time "raw" data during an emergency and the time required to properly quality control (QC) this data [24], [25]. Time constraints often force modellers to use unverified raw data, highlighting a need for better processes to define uncertainty ranges so that data limitations are understood in subsequent modelling [26], [27].
*   **Accuracy vs. Investment:** Researchers are investigating whether the industry is overinvesting in capturing highly accurate measurable data, such as floor levels. Statistical assessments are needed to determine exactly what level of floor-level accuracy is actually required to reliably calculate flood damages without wasting resources [28].

**Management Options**
*   **Natural Floodplain Management:** There is a lack of understanding of natural biophysical processes (such as soil infiltration rates, vegetation interception, and log jams) which leads to a preference for hard engineering solutions. More biophysical data is needed to integrate natural mitigation techniques into hydraulic models [29], [30].
*   **"Room for the River" Strategies:** Instead of simply raising levees—which can transfer risk downstream—future strategies should investigate restoring natural floodplain storages to safely hold water away from urban areas [31], [32], [33].
*   **Holistic Options in Developing Nations:** In Pacific Island Countries, there is a gap in risk-informed land use planning. Current practices show an over-reliance on structural works like dredging and levees. Priorities include establishing holistic governance to evaluate a broader suite of non-structural FRM measures and understanding the root causes of floodplain exposure [34], [35], [36].
