**Bureau of Meteorology (BoM) and Modelling Directions**
Research indicates a critical need to transition from conventional deterministic static modelling towards more **flexible, integrated, and adaptive model structures** that account for human impacts on hydrological systems [1], [2]. Specific future directions and gaps include:
*   **Ensemble Prediction and Uncertainty:** There is a need to better quantify forecast uncertainty. The BoM is developing formal ensemble numerical weather prediction (NWP) models (like ACCESS-GE) and statistical post-processing methods to generate reliable rainfall forecasts and reduce model over- or under-confidence [3].
*   **Technological Integration:** Future research should investigate how to use **artificial intelligence and big data** to combine modelled outputs, historical data, and social media for rapid warning updates during fast-evolving floods [4]. 
*   **Non-stationarity and Ungauged Basins:** Researchers highlight the need to incorporate non-stationarity in modelling due to climate and land-use changes, and to improve coverage of ungauged basins using platforms like SMART [4].
*   **Impact-based Warnings:** Developing integrated impact-based warnings that combine advanced hydraulic modelling with local flood intelligence to better communicate specific risk levels to communities [5].

**Social Impact Analysis and Psycho-social Issues**
Current guidelines often oversimplify social impacts. Authors point out that a much deeper understanding of disaster social sciences is required to evaluate floodplain management options holistically [6]. Identified research questions and gaps include:
*   **Cognitive and Behavioral Factors:** There is a need to understand the **psychology of risk aversion** and what factors lead to "cognitive dissonance" or illogical behavior in flood-prone areas [7], [8]. Research must also address "Optimism Bias" and its impact on emergency management planning [9].
*   **Health and Well-being:** More investigation is needed on how floods affect physical and mental health across different demographic and socioeconomic groups over time, including how the duration of displacement influences the severity of these health impacts [10]. Methods are being developed to quantify mental health costs, such as stress and anxiety [11].
*   **Unintended Consequences of Mitigation:** Assessing complex social costs of structural options, such as the **"levee paradox"** (where levees reduce preparedness and increase risk perception issues), visual amenity impacts, and equity issues for those living outside levees [12], [6].
*   **Community Integration:** Investigating the role of citizen science and indigenous knowledge in flood monitoring, and finding the best ways to communicate appropriately worded warning messages to vulnerable groups [2], [13].

**Economic Assessment Framework**
A significant gap exists in how the economic benefits of floodplain risk management (FRM) projects are calculated. Current cost-benefit analyses (CBAs) are often overly conservative because they primarily focus on direct property-related damages [14], [15]. Future research and framework developments should address:
*   **Valuing Intangible Costs:** There is a need to quantify intangible and non-property damages in dollar terms, such as the risk to life, social disruption, amenity loss, and mental health impacts, across a range of flood events (from 20-year ARI to the Probable Maximum Flood) [16], [17], [18]. 
*   **Rapid Assessment Techniques:** Because many FRM projects have capital costs below $1 million, undertaking highly detailed CBAs is often hard to justify. Research is currently underway to develop **"rapid" evaluation techniques** for assessing non-direct property damages efficiently [19], [20].
*   **Climate Resilience Dividends:** Existing funding evaluations fail to adequately capture the present value of mitigating *future-climate* risks. Incorporating this "climate resilience dividend" could make community-scale adaptation projects or planned retreats economically viable in highly climate-sensitive areas [21], [22].

**Data and Quality Assurance**
The increasing resolution of flood models (e.g., transitioning from 1D to highly detailed 2D grids) and the frequent updating of topography and built environments have created a **"big flood data" problem** [23], [24], [25], [26], [27]. Areas needing more investigation include:
*   **Data Management Frameworks:** Developing best-practice frameworks for record-keeping, dataset tracking, archiving, handling study overlap, and allowing "point-in-time" data rollbacks (critical for legal challenges) [28], [29], [6].
*   **Raw vs. Quality-Controlled Data:** There is a gap in understanding the limitations of using near-real-time "raw" data post-flood. Time constraints and political pressure often force modellers to use raw data, but proper quality control is required to define uncertainty and true water levels [30], [31], [32].
*   **Floor Level Accuracy vs. Value:** The industry is heavily investing in capturing extensive building floor level data. Future statistical assessments need to determine exactly **what level of accuracy in floor levels is actually required** to calculate reliable economic flood damages, and how errors compound with stage-damage curves [33].
*   **Nationally Consistent Data:** There is a glaring lack of centralized, nationally consistent hazard data to form the basis of whole-of-community current and future climate risk assessments [34], [22].
*   **Biophysical Data:** A lack of biophysical data in floodplains (e.g., soil infiltration rates, interception, stem flow, vegetation impacts) limits the ability to model and implement natural flood mitigation strategies effectively [35].

**Management Options and Mitigation**
Future floodplain management requires moving beyond traditional hard-engineering solutions towards holistic, inter-disciplinary systems [36]. Future directions include:
*   **Natural Floodplain Management:** Research is exploring how to actively engage natural systems—such as **green infrastructure and natural capital**—to supplement engineering. This involves understanding the surface-groundwater interface, soil health, and maximizing the hydrological benefits of increased vegetation cover to reduce overland flow [37], [9], [35], [38].
*   **"Room for the River":** Investigating spatial strategies, like altering existing levee layouts, to increase the utilization of floodplain storage capacity and improve urban flood immunity [39].
*   **Systematic Adaptability:** Integrating formerly disparate concepts like water-sensitive urban design, ecosystem services, and livability into fundamental floodplain management. This includes evaluating options for the strategic adaptation of existing schemes (e.g., Hunter Valley Mitigation Scheme) to remain fit-for-purpose under future climate change scenarios [40], [41], [42], [43].
