The HWRS 2022 sources extensively discuss climate change and the profound uncertainties it introduces to hydrology and water resources management. **A central theme across the symposium is the transition away from traditional, backward-looking models toward adaptive planning frameworks that can handle unpredictable, dynamic climate futures** [1], [2], [3].

**The Death of Stationarity and "Deep Uncertainty"**
Historically, the planning and design of water resource infrastructure relied on the assumption of "stationarity"—the idea that the future hydrologic behavior of a catchment would mirror its past [4], [5]. However, **the sources emphasize that due to anthropogenic climate change, the stationarity assumption is "dead"** [4], [6]. Relinquishing this assumption introduces what planners classify as **"deep uncertainty"**, a state where multiple plausible futures exist and outcomes cannot be accurately predicted using current historical knowledge alone [7], [8].

**Specific Sources of Climate Uncertainty**
The sources identify several key areas where climate change drives high uncertainty in hydrological modeling:
*   **Rainfall vs. Temperature Projections:** While there is very high confidence in warming temperature trends, predictions for future rainfall—including its intensity, total volume, and spatial distribution—are highly inconsistent [9], [10], [11]. Climate models demonstrate high skill in predicting historical temperatures but notably low skill in predicting rainfall [12].
*   **Catchment Response Shifts:** Prolonged droughts, exacerbated by climate change, can trigger fundamental shifts in how catchments partition precipitation into runoff and evapotranspiration [13], [14]. As a result, reductions in runoff can be disproportionately larger than the relative reductions in rainfall [14].
*   **Model and Downscaling Limitations:** There are considerable uncertainties inherent in General Circulation Models (GCMs) and Regional Climate Models (RCMs) [15], [16]. These uncertainties stem from unknown future greenhouse gas emissions, inaccurate representations of localized climate processes, and the complexity of downscaling coarse climate data to local catchment levels [17], [18].

**Impacts on Infrastructure and Water Security**
The convergence of these uncertainties significantly impacts both infrastructure design and water supply security:
*   **Flooding and Multi-Hazard Disasters:** Climate change is predicted to alter rainfall patterns, leading to more frequent and intense extreme weather events [19], [20]. Rapid shifts in weather conditions can create complex, multi-hazard disasters that modern civil infrastructure (like dams, levees, and bridges) may be inadequately designed to withstand [21], [22]. 
*   **Water Security and Yield:** Estimating the reliable "yield" of a water supply is increasingly difficult, forcing planners to evaluate the heightened risk of long-term supply shortfalls [23], [24]. For example, relying solely on historical climate data to assess urban water security may dangerously underestimate the risk of severe supply shortages, necessitating models that account for extended and extreme drought scenarios [25], [26].

**Strategies for Managing Uncertainty**
To manage deep uncertainties without succumbing to "decision paralysis," the HWRS 2022 papers propose several modern methodologies:
*   **Envelopes of Emission Scenarios:** Rather than relying on a single forecast, practitioners are advised to use multiple Representative Concentration Pathways (typically RCP 4.5 for moderate mitigation and RCP 8.5 for "business as usual") across an ensemble of climate models to establish a realistic envelope of possible climate futures [27], [28], [29], [30], [31].
*   **Scenario-Neutral Stress Testing:** Many planners are adopting scenario-neutral approaches, which involve stress-testing a water system against a broad range of perturbed climate attributes (like varying rainfall and evaporation rates) to identify failure modes before assigning probabilities to those failures [3], [32], [33].
*   **Stochastic Modelling and 'Operational Yield':** Generating synthetic, stochastic climate data helps quantify the risks of rare extreme events outside the historical record [34], [35]. Furthermore, transitioning toward an "operational yield" approach—which continuously updates system models based on current storage conditions and near-term demand—provides a better framework for adaptive planning than rigid, long-term yield estimates [36], [37]. 
*   **Cross-Disciplinary Collaboration:** Addressing the multi-hazard risks of climate change requires a deeper merging of civil engineering with atmospheric science, ensuring that infrastructure design accounts for complex, dynamic weather processes rather than relying solely on historical statistics [21], [20].
